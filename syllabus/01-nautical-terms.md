# Nautical Terms

## Summary
This topic covers the essential vocabulary of a sailing yacht: hull geometry, structural parts above and below deck, the standing and running rigging, points of sailing relative to the wind, and the main rig types encountered on cruising and racing yachts. Proficiency with these terms is a prerequisite for every other aspect of the Day Skipper syllabus.

## Key Facts

### Hull and Deck Geometry
- The **bow** is the forward end of the vessel; the **stern** is the aft end. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- **Port** is the left side of the vessel when facing forward; **starboard** is the right side. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- **Beam** refers both to the width of the vessel and to the athwartships direction; "on the beam" means 90° to the centreline. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- **Amidships** means in the middle of the vessel, either longitudinally or athwartships. [Source: SRC-001]
- **Freeboard** is the height of the hull side above the waterline. [Source: SRC-001]
- **Draught** (draft) is the depth of the keel below the waterline — the minimum depth of water the vessel needs to float. [Source: SRC-001]
- **LOA** (length overall) is the total length of the hull from bow to stern. [Source: SRC-001]
- **LWL** (length at waterline) is the length of the hull at the waterline. [Source: SRC-001]
- The **transom** is the flat or curved vertical panel forming the stern of the vessel. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- **Topsides** are the sides of the hull between the waterline and the deck. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **waterline** marks the point where the hull meets the water surface. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **keel** provides ballast and lateral resistance to leeway; types include the **fin keel** (short and deep, modern cruising yachts) and the **long keel** (traditional, more of the hull bottom). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- A **fin and bulb keel** is used on sports/racing boats — a narrow fin with a heavy bulb at the base for stability. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- The **rudder** is the steering foil, controlled via the tiller or wheel; a **spade rudder** is hung from the keel or skeg only (no hull support at the bottom). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]

### Above Deck — Deck Fittings
- The **pulpit** is the fixed stainless-steel guard rail frame at the bow; the **pushpit** is the equivalent frame at the stern. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- **Stanchions** support the **guard rails** (lifelines) along the sides of the deck. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- A **jackstay** is a strong wire or webbing running fore-and-aft on deck used to clip on safety tethers. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **coach roof** (coachroof) is the raised cabin top providing headroom below deck. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **companionway** is the main entrance hatch leading below deck, usually with a **sliding hatch** and washboards. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- A **windlass** is a winch mounted horizontally on the foredeck used to raise and lower the anchor. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **mainsheet traveller** is a track allowing the mainsheet to be adjusted athwartships. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **cockpit locker** provides storage accessed from the cockpit; the **cockpit sole** is the cockpit floor. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- A **binnacle** is the pedestal housing the steering compass. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]

### Standing Rigging
- The **forestay** is the wire running from the masthead (or near it) to the bow, supporting the mast forward and on which the headsail is set. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- The **backstay** runs from the masthead to the stern, supporting the mast aft. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- **Shrouds** are the side wires supporting the mast athwartships; **spreaders** project athwartships from the mast to spread the shroud angle. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- A **bottle screw** (turnbuckle) is a tensioning fitting used to adjust the standing rigging. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]

### Running Rigging and Sails
- The **boom** is the horizontal spar attached to the mast at the **gooseneck** fitting, forming the foot of the mainsail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]
- The **kicking strap** (vang) prevents the boom from rising and keeps sail shape when running. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2 & p.5]
- A **halyard** raises a sail; a **sheet** controls a sail's angle to the wind. [Source: SRC-001]
- The **topping lift** supports the boom when the mainsail is lowered. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- **Sail battens** are flexible strips inserted in pockets in the leech of the mainsail to support the roach. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- The parts of a sail: **luff** (leading edge), **leech** (trailing edge), **foot** (bottom edge), **tack** (lower forward corner), **clew** (lower aft corner), **head** (top corner). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- A **roller furling genoa** rolls around the forestay for easy stowage. **Reef points** and **reefing cringles** allow the mainsail to be reefed (reduced in area). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- **Jammers** hold rope under load like a cleat; winch **selftailers** grip the rope automatically. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2]

### Below Deck
- The **forecabin** (or V-berth) is the forward sleeping cabin; the **vee berth** is the V-shaped double berth in it. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **chain locker** stores the anchor chain forward. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **head** is the onboard toilet compartment; **skin fittings** are through-hull valves. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **saloon** is the main living area; **settee berths** convert to sleeping berths. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **galley** is the onboard kitchen; the **cabin sole** is the interior floor; the **bilge** is the space beneath the sole. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **deckhead** is the interior ceiling (underside of the deck). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **chart table** is a dedicated navigation workspace fitted with plotting instruments. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]
- The **aft cabin** or **quarter berth** is the sleeping area aft of the main saloon. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.3]

### Points of Sailing
- A boat cannot sail closer than approximately **45° to the wind** — the arc ahead is the **no-go zone** (head to wind/in irons). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- To make progress to windward, the boat must **tack** — sailing a series of zigzags, each approximately 45° to the wind. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Close-hauled** — sailing as close to the wind as possible, sails pulled in tight (~45° to the true wind). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Beam reach** — wind coming directly from the side (90° to the centreline). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Broad reach** — wind coming from behind and to the side (~135° to the centreline). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Dead run** (running before the wind) — wind directly from astern (180°). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **In irons** — the boat is head to wind and has stopped; it cannot sail in this position. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Tacking** is turning the bow through the wind to change tacks; **gybing** is turning the stern through the wind. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- **Luffing up** means turning towards the wind; **bearing away** means turning away from the wind. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- On **starboard tack** the wind comes over the starboard side; on **port tack** it comes over the port side. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]

### Rig Types
- A **modern cruising yacht** is typically a Bermudian (Marconi) sloop with a fin keel and roller-furling headsail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- A **gaff rig** uses a gaff spar at the top of the mainsail (the peak is the top aft corner, the throat the top forward corner). A **gaff-rigged cutter** carries a bowsprit, jib, staysail, and gaff mainsail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]
- A **spinnaker** is a large, lightweight downwind sail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5]

### Sailing Theory
- When air flows over a sail it creates a **lift force** (like a wing); combined with the water's sideways resistance this drives the boat forward. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]
- A sailing boat resists heeling because the **ballast keel** weight and **buoyancy** counteract the overturning force of the wind. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4]

### Mooring Alongside and in a Pen

#### Alongside a Wall or Quay
- Be aware of the **rise and fall of tide** when mooring alongside a wall — lines must be long enough and adjustable as the tide changes. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- Use **stretchy nylon warps** alongside a wall to absorb the movement of the boat. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- Length of warps should be at least **4× the rise and fall of tide** to allow adjustment without re-rigging. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- When you attach a warp to a bollard or mooring ring ashore, bring the rest of the line back on board and attach it to a cleat so that each warp can be **independently adjusted from on board** as the tide rises and falls. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- Run lines through a **fairlead** and then onto a cleat. Use **separate lines for each different task** (bow line, stern line, springs). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- Standard mooring lines: **bow line** (forward), **stern line** (aft), **bow spring** (runs aft from bow to prevent forward movement), **stern spring** (runs forward from stern to prevent aft movement). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7 & p.8]
- A **bowline** knot is used to make a loop for placing over a bollard or ring. **Round turn and two half hitches** is used to secure a line to a ring or rail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]

#### Mooring in a Raft
- When two or more boats moor alongside each other (a **raft**), attach **breast ropes** and **springs** between vessels at bow and stern. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- The **outside boat** should take bow and stern lines ashore to minimise the strain on the inside boat's shore lines. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]
- Fenders should be deployed between all vessels in the raft. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.7]

#### On a Floating Pontoon
- When mooring on a floating pontoon, lines do **not** need to be adjusted as the tide rises and falls because the pontoon rises with the tide. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- A single long line can serve double duty (e.g., as both bow line and forespring). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- Standard pontoon mooring uses: **stern line**, **stern spring** (back spring), **bow line**, **bow spring** (forespring). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- A **finger pontoon** is a short pontoon projecting from the main pontoon alongside which a single vessel moors. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]

#### In a Pen (Between Piles)
- In a **pen**, stern lines are attached to piles and bow lines are attached to the pontoon or dock at the shore end. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- Usually the **windward lines are attached first**. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- **Pick-up lines** are usually supplied on the piles to aid retrieval of the mooring lines from the water. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]
- Some pens have a **ring on the pile that rises and falls with the tide** — the line passes through this ring so no adjustment is needed. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.8]

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What does "draught" mean? | A: The depth of the keel below the waterline — the minimum water depth needed to float the vessel | Source: SRC-001
- Q: Name the three edges of a sail | A: Luff (leading edge), leech (trailing edge), foot (bottom edge) | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5
- Q: What is the difference between tacking and gybing? | A: Tacking turns the bow through the wind; gybing turns the stern through the wind | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.4
- Q: What is a jackstay? | A: A strong fore-and-aft wire or webbing on deck used to attach safety tethers | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2
- Q: What is the function of the kicking strap (vang)? | A: Prevents the boom from rising and controls sail shape, particularly when running | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.2 & p.5
- Q: What is the difference between a fin keel and a long keel? | A: A fin keel is short and deep (modern cruising); a long keel runs most of the hull length (traditional) | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.5
