# Ropework

## Summary
This topic covers the properties and construction of synthetic ropes used aboard sailing yachts, the correct techniques for securing ropes to cleats and winches, and the principal knots used in sailing with their specific applications. Understanding which knot to use in which situation is a practical safety skill as well as a knowledge-test topic.

## Key Facts

### Rope Materials
- **Polyester** (Dacron) is the most common yacht rope material: strong, low-stretch, UV-resistant, sinks. Used for sheets and halyards. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Nylon** has significant stretch (elasticity), making it ideal for mooring lines and anchor warps where shock absorption is needed. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Polypropylene** is lightweight and floats, but has low UV resistance and limited strength. Used for throw lines and dinghy painters. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]

### Rope Construction
- **Laid (twisted) rope** has three strands twisted together; traditional construction, easier to splice but more stretch. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Braided rope** has a braided outer sheath over a core; more comfortable to handle, better for sheets and control lines. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]

### Making Fast to a Cleat
- Lead the rope to the **open side** of the cleat, take a full turn around the base, then make several **figure-of-eight** turns, and finish with one more round turn to increase friction. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- Always secure to the open (non-loaded) side of the cleat to allow easy release. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]

### Winching Techniques
- Always wind rope **clockwise** around a winch drum. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- Keep the rope lead **low** to avoid a riding turn (a jammed, crossed turn that can be dangerous to release). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- Use the **selftailer** to grip the rope before winching. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- Turn the winch handle **either way** to pull in rope. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- To let out under load: ease the rope slowly around the drum using the **palm of the hand**; never let go suddenly. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Letting fly** (releasing quickly): flip the rope off the top of the drum — keep fingers clear of the gap between rope and drum. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- A **jammer** holds a rope under load like a cleat. When releasing a rope under load from a jammer, always take the strain on a winch first — never hold the rope close to the jammer. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]

### Knots and Their Applications
- **Bowline** — makes a fixed, non-slipping loop (eye) in a rope. Secure under tension. Uses: attaching jib sheets to the clew, loops in mooring lines, rescue work. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Cleat hitch** (making fast to a cleat) — figure-of-eight turns on a cleat; the standard method for securing a line to a cleat. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Round turn and two half hitches** — multipurpose hitch tied to a ring, rail, or post. Can be untied while under tension. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Figure of eight** — stopper knot tied in the end of a sheet or halyard to prevent it running out through a block or fairlead. Easy to tie and untie. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Reef knot** — joins two ends of the same rope (e.g., tying in reef points). Not secure for joining two different ropes or for critical loads. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Clove hitch** — quick to tie and easy to adjust; ideal for securing fenders to a guard rail. Less secure than other hitches under variable load. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Rolling hitch** — used when a knot must not slip along a rope or spar when pulled at an angle; ideal for taking the strain off another rope (e.g., to free a riding turn). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]
- **Sheet bend** — joins two ropes, especially of different diameters. A **double sheet bend** is more secure for very different rope sizes. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6]

## Flashcard Candidates
- Q: Which rope material is best for mooring lines and anchor warps, and why? | A: Nylon — its elasticity absorbs shock loads | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: What knot is used to make a fixed, non-slipping loop in a rope? | A: Bowline | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: Which knot should be used to secure a fender to a guard rail? | A: Clove hitch — quick to tie and easy to adjust | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: What is a rolling hitch used for? | A: Taking the strain off a loaded rope or spar when the pull is at an angle | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: Why is a reef knot not suitable for joining two ropes of different diameters? | A: It is not secure when used for two ropes of different sizes or under varying load; it can capsize | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: What must you do before releasing a rope under load from a jammer? | A: Take the strain on a winch first — never hold the rope close to the jammer | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
- Q: In which direction must rope be wound around a winch drum? | A: Clockwise | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6

## MCQ Candidates

Q: Which rope material floats and is therefore useful for throw lines?
(a) Polyester
(b) Nylon
(c) Polypropylene
(d) Dyneema
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6

Q: Which knot creates a fixed eye in a rope and remains secure under tension?
(a) Reef knot
(b) Bowline
(c) Clove hitch
(d) Sheet bend
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6

Q: A rolling hitch is most useful when:
(a) Joining two ropes of different diameters
(b) Making fast to a cleat quickly
(c) Taking the strain off a loaded rope pulled at an angle
(d) Stopping a rope from running through a block
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6

Q: What is a riding turn on a winch, and how is it best prevented?
(a) A figure-of-eight wrap; prevented by using a jammer
(b) A crossed turn that jams under load; prevented by keeping the rope lead low
(c) A turn used to secure the selftailer; prevented by using nylon rope
(d) An extra half hitch; prevented by winding anticlockwise
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6

Q: Which knot is used to join two ropes, particularly ropes of different diameters?
(a) Reef knot
(b) Rolling hitch
(c) Sheet bend
(d) Clove hitch
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.6
