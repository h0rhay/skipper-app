# Anchorwork

## Summary
This topic covers the main anchor types used on cruising yachts, the principles of scope calculation for chain and warp, how to select a suitable anchorage, and the practical considerations for anchoring safely including swinging room, seabed type, and tidal awareness. Correct anchoring technique is important both for safety and for environmental reasons.

## Key Facts

### Scope
- **Scope** is the length of anchor chain or warp paid out; it determines the angle of pull on the anchor and greatly affects holding power. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- For **chain**: scope should be at least **4 × the maximum depth of water** expected during the stay. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- For **warp** (rope anchor rode): scope should be at least **6 × the maximum depth of water** expected. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- Depth used for scope calculation must account for **maximum tidal height** during the stay, not just the depth at the time of anchoring. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]

### Anchor Types
- **Bruce (claw) anchor** — good holding-to-weight ratio; awkward to stow in a small anchor locker due to its three-dimensional claw shape. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Delta anchor** — good holding-to-weight ratio; designed to remain on the bow roller for self-launching; rigid (no moving parts). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Danforth (fluke) anchor** — good holding-to-weight ratio; stows flat; can be hard to break out (recover) from mud. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **CQR (plough) anchor** — good holding-to-weight ratio; has a hinged joint (moving parts) that can capsize the anchor; hard to stow on deck. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Fisherman's anchor** — traditional stock-and-flukes design; good for rocky and weedy bottoms; poor holding power in sand and mud; awkward to stow. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]

### Selecting an Anchorage
- **Shelter**: seek protection from wind, swell, and tidal stream. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Seabed**: check the chart symbols — **mud and sand** give better holding than rock or shingle. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Tidal effect**: calculate tide times and heights; ensure the boat will not ground as the tide falls. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Swinging room**: allow adequate room for the vessel to swing through 360° around the anchor as wind and tide change; consider other vessels, isolated rocks, and shallow patches. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- Light or flat-bottomed boats (high windage) will lie differently from deeper-draught, low-windage boats — mixed fleets can have collision risk on a swinging anchorage. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- **Prepare** the required length of chain or warp before dropping the anchor. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- Avoid anchoring on or near a **leading line** — other vessels may be using it to enter harbour. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- The chart's anchor symbol is a recommendation only — anchoring anywhere in the bay that meets the above criteria is acceptable. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]
- Tidal flow will generally be less inside a bay than in open water outside it. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Scope | Length of anchor rode (chain or warp) paid out | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Anchor rode | The chain or warp connecting the anchor to the vessel | SRC-001 |
| Warp | Rope used as an anchor rode (as opposed to chain) | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Chain | Metal link cable used as anchor rode; heavy, helps hold angle low | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Bruce anchor | Three-claw anchor with good holding power; difficult to stow | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Delta anchor | Rigid plough-type anchor suitable for bow roller; self-launches | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Danforth anchor | Twin-fluke flat anchor; stows easily; can foul in mud | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| CQR anchor | Hinged plough-type anchor; can capsize due to moving parts | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Fisherman's anchor | Traditional stock anchor; suits rocky/weedy seabeds | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Swinging room | Clear water radius needed for the vessel to swing around its anchor | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Holding ground | The quality of the seabed for anchor holding (sand/mud good; rock/shingle poor) | SRC-001; transcript p.9 |
| Breaking out | Recovering (weighing) an anchor from the seabed | SRC-001 |

## Flashcard Candidates
- Q: What scope is recommended for a chain anchor rode? | A: Minimum 4 × the maximum depth of water during the stay | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: What scope is recommended for a warp anchor rode? | A: Minimum 6 × the maximum depth of water during the stay | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: Which anchor type is best suited to rocky or weedy seabeds? | A: Fisherman's anchor | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: Why is the Danforth anchor sometimes difficult to recover? | A: It can be hard to break out of mud | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: What two seabed types give the best holding for most anchors? | A: Mud and sand | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: Why must maximum tidal depth (not current depth) be used for scope calculation? | A: The depth increases with the rising tide; insufficient scope at high water could cause the anchor to drag | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
- Q: Why should you not anchor on a leading line? | A: Other vessels may be using it to navigate into harbour and could run you down | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9

## MCQ Candidates

Q: A boat anchors in 3 metres of water using chain. What is the minimum scope recommended?
(a) 6 metres
(b) 9 metres
(c) 12 metres
(d) 18 metres
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9

Q: A boat anchors in 4 metres using warp. What minimum length of warp should be paid out?
(a) 12 metres
(b) 16 metres
(c) 20 metres
(d) 24 metres
Correct: d
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9

Q: Which anchor is designed to remain on the bow roller for self-launching and has no moving parts?
(a) CQR plough
(b) Fisherman's
(c) Delta
(d) Danforth
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9

Q: Which type of holding ground gives the poorest anchor hold for most modern anchors?
(a) Sand
(b) Mud
(c) Rock
(d) Silt
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9

Q: When anchoring in a mixed anchorage, why might boats of different types collide even when none drag their anchors?
(a) They use different scope ratios
(b) Boats with high windage swing differently from deep-draught, low-windage boats
(c) Chain rodes are shorter than warp rodes
(d) All boats face the same direction in all conditions
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.9
