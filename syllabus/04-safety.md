# Topic 4 — Safety

## Summary

Safety is a foundational topic in the RYA Day Skipper syllabus. It covers the equipment required on board a pleasure vessel, personal protective gear for the crew, fire prevention and fighting, and what to do in an emergency. This includes alerting the coastguard via VHF, using distress flares correctly, abandoning to a liferaft, and working with rescue services (RNLI lifeboats and coastguard helicopters).

A skipper is responsible for ensuring suitable safety equipment is carried, that crew know where it is and how to use it, and that appropriate precautions are taken before leaving harbour. The MCA's MGN 280 (M+F) sets out safety equipment recommendations for pleasure vessels in UK waters.

Key themes: layered personal protection (clothing, lifejacket, harness, jackstay), fire triangle precautions, structured distress communications (Mayday/Pan Pan), and the principle of staying with the vessel until abandonment is absolutely necessary.

## Key Facts

- A lifejacket should be carried for every person on board. [Source: SRC-005, MGN 280 — general safety equipment guidance]
- A gas-inflation lifejacket with an integral harness provides both buoyancy and a means of tethering the crew to the vessel. [Source: transcript page-11]
- Harnesses should be worn in bad weather and when going forward on deck at night. [Source: transcript page-10]
- A jackstay is a line rigged along the deck to which a harness tether is clipped, allowing a crew member to move fore and aft while remaining attached to the boat. [Source: transcript page-10, page-11]
- Larger ships (over 50 m) must carry two steaming lights (forward masthead lights at different heights). [Source: transcript page-17]
- Fire extinguisher types carried on board should be matched to the fire risk: dry powder (not for flammable liquids), CO₂/halocarbon (good for enclosed spaces such as the engine bay), AFFF foam (good for flammable liquids). [Source: transcript page-12]
- A fire blanket is essential in the galley — it can smother a cooking fire and is also effective if clothing is on fire. [Source: transcript page-10, page-12]
- The engine space should have a dedicated extinguisher that is automatic or can be activated remotely without opening the engine compartment (which would introduce oxygen and feed the fire). [Source: transcript page-12]
- When fighting any fire, aim the extinguisher at the base of the flames. [Source: transcript page-12]
- Escaping LPG (gas) is heavier than air and sinks into the bilges; to clear it, open hatches and turn downwind to vent fresh air through the boat. [Source: transcript page-12]
- Always vent the engine space before starting an inboard petrol engine to prevent ignition of accumulated vapour. [Source: transcript page-12]
- Keep outboard motors on deck (not stored below) to avoid the build-up of petrol vapour below decks. [Source: transcript page-12]
- Gas cylinders must be stored in a sealed locker that drains overboard. [Source: transcript page-12]
- A VHF radio is preferable to a mobile phone in an emergency: a VHF call alerts all vessels within range, allows the coastguard to take a radio direction-finding fix on the vessel's position, and enables direct communication with a lifeboat or helicopter. [Source: transcript page-14]
- A DSC-equipped VHF set can send a distress alert at the press of a button; if linked to GPS it will automatically include the vessel's position in the alert. [Source: transcript page-14]
- Mayday is the distress signal used when life or vessel are in grave and imminent danger. The call includes: "Mayday" × 3, vessel name × 3, "Mayday" + vessel name, MMSI (if DSC-fitted), position, nature of distress, number of persons on board, "Over". [Source: transcript page-14]
- Pan Pan is the urgency signal used when crew or vessel need assistance but the situation is not immediately life-threatening. [Source: transcript page-14]
- A Mayday call may be made on VHF without an operator's certificate; otherwise an operator's certificate is required or the radio must be used under the supervision of a qualified person. [Source: transcript page-14]
- When abandoning to a liferaft: throw the raft to windward, tug the painter to inflate, board from the yacht (stay dry), put the heaviest/strongest crew in first to stabilise, then close the door, stream the drogue, and ventilate every hour. [Source: transcript page-13]
- During a helicopter rescue, the vessel must steer a very steady course — typically with the wind 30° on the port bow. Listen to instructions given on VHF before the helicopter arrives, as it will be too noisy overhead. A weighted line may be lowered first — gather in slack but do not attach it to the boat. [Source: transcript page-13]
- Common causes of fire on board: smoking below decks, solvents/paints stored below, faulty wiring, cooking fats. [Source: transcript page-12]
- Symptoms of hypothermia: shivering, pale colour, irrational/disorientated behaviour. [Source: transcript page-11]
- Bilge pumps are designed to pump water and will not clear petrol/gas vapour effectively. [Source: transcript page-12]
- An EPIRB (Emergency Position-Indicating Radio Beacon) and PLB (Personal Locator Beacon) are electronic distress devices; they transmit the vessel's or individual's position to search-and-rescue services via satellite. [Source: transcript page-10 (EPIRB listed as safety equipment); classification/detail — see Unverified]
- A radar reflector should be fitted to improve the vessel's detectability on other ships' radar. [Source: transcript page-10]
- A horseshoe lifebelt with drogue and floating light should be carried and mounted where it can be deployed immediately for a person in the water. [Source: transcript page-10]
- Softwood bungs should be kept near each through-hull fitting (skin fitting) so they can be used to plug a broken fitting and prevent flooding. [Source: transcript page-10]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|------------|--------|
| Lifejacket | Personal buoyancy device designed to turn an unconscious person face-up in the water and keep the airway clear. Usually gas-inflation type. | transcript page-10, page-11 |
| Buoyancy aid | Lower-buoyancy device (typically foam) that assists a conscious swimmer but does not turn an unconscious person face-up. Suitable for inshore/dinghy sailing. | transcript page-11 (implied by contrast with lifejacket) |
| Harness | Webbing worn over the body with strong attachment points; connected by a tether (harness line) to a jackstay or strong point to prevent a crew member going overboard. | transcript page-11 |
| Jackstay | A wire or webbing line rigged along the deck (port and starboard) to which harness tethers are clipped, allowing crew to move safely on deck in heavy weather. | transcript page-10, page-11 |
| EPIRB | Emergency Position-Indicating Radio Beacon. A vessel-mounted device that, when activated (automatically on immersion or manually), transmits a distress signal with GPS position via satellite to COSPAS-SARSAT. | transcript page-10 |
| PLB | Personal Locator Beacon. A smaller, personal version of an EPIRB, carried by an individual crew member. | general knowledge — see Unverified |
| Liferaft | An inflatable survival raft, stored in a canister on deck, deployed if the vessel must be abandoned. Equipped with a canopy, drogue, and survival equipment. | transcript page-10, page-13 |
| Painter (liferaft) | The line connecting the liferaft canister to the vessel; when pulled, it triggers the inflation mechanism. | transcript page-13 |
| Drogue | A sea anchor or drag device streamed from the liferaft to slow drift and reduce the risk of capsizing. | transcript page-13 |
| AFFF | Aqueous Film-Forming Foam — fire extinguisher medium effective against flammable liquid fires (Class B). | transcript page-12 |
| CO₂/Halocarbon extinguisher | Fire extinguisher that smothers flames by displacing oxygen; effective in enclosed spaces such as the engine bay; leaves no residue. | transcript page-12 |
| Mayday | International voice distress signal (from French "m'aidez" — help me) indicating grave and imminent danger to life or vessel. Transmitted on VHF Ch 16. | transcript page-14 |
| Pan Pan | International urgency signal indicating a serious situation requiring assistance but not immediate danger to life. | transcript page-14 |
| DSC | Digital Selective Calling — a feature of modern VHF radios allowing a digital distress alert (including MMSI and, if GPS-linked, position) to be sent at the press of a button. | transcript page-14 |
| MMSI | Maritime Mobile Service Identity — a unique 9-digit number assigned to a DSC-equipped vessel; included in DSC distress calls. | transcript page-14 |
| Parachute flare | A rocket-launched red flare that deploys a parachute, burning for approximately 40 seconds at altitude for maximum range and visibility; long-range distress signal. | transcript page-10, page-14 |
| Hand flare (red) | A handheld red flare used to pinpoint the vessel's position at short range, inshore or when rescuers are already in sight. | transcript page-10, page-14 |
| Orange smoke | Daytime distress/position signal — canister (floating or handheld) producing dense orange smoke visible from air and sea. | transcript page-14 |
| Skin fitting | A through-hull fitting below the waterline; if it fails, the vessel will flood. Should be adjacent to a softwood bung of matching size. | transcript page-10 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: When should a harness be worn on deck? | A: In bad weather and when going forward on deck at night. | Source: transcript page-10
- Q: What is the correct sequence for a Mayday voice call? | A: "Mayday, Mayday, Mayday — This is [vessel name] × 3 — Mayday [vessel name] — position — nature of distress — persons on board — Over." | Source: transcript page-14
- Q: What is the difference between a Mayday and a Pan Pan call? | A: Mayday = grave and imminent danger to life or vessel. Pan Pan = urgent situation requiring assistance, but not immediately life-threatening. | Source: transcript page-14
- Q: What type of fire extinguisher is best for an engine space? | A: CO₂ or halocarbon — effective in enclosed spaces; ideally automatic or remotely activated so the compartment is not opened and oxygen is not introduced. | Source: transcript page-12
- Q: Why is VHF better than a mobile phone in a distress situation? | A: VHF alerts all vessels within range, can be used by coastguard to take a direction-finding fix, and allows direct communication with lifeboat and helicopter. | Source: transcript page-14
- Q: How do you clear petrol/LPG vapour from below decks? | A: Open hatches and turn downwind to vent fresh air through the boat. Do NOT use the bilge pump — it will not clear gas effectively. | Source: transcript page-12
- Q: When abandoning to a liferaft, which crew member should board first? | A: The heaviest and strongest, to stabilise the raft and assist others in boarding. | Source: transcript page-13
- Q: What course should the helm steer during a helicopter rescue? | A: A very steady course, typically with the wind 30° on the port bow. | Source: transcript page-13
- Q: What are the three types of fire extinguisher described for use on yachts, and what is each used for? | A: (1) Dry powder — general use, not for flammable liquids. (2) CO₂/halocarbon — enclosed spaces/engine. (3) AFFF foam — flammable liquids. | Source: transcript page-12
- Q: What distress signal should NEVER be fired if a helicopter is approaching? | A: A parachute (rocket) flare — it could strike the aircraft. | Source: transcript page-14

## Safety-Critical Notes

- NEVER fire a parachute flare if a helicopter is approaching — it could strike the aircraft and cause a catastrophe.
- NEVER fire a flare into the wind.
- Do NOT attach a helicopter's weighted guide line to any part of the vessel.
- Do NOT open the engine compartment to fight a fire — this introduces oxygen. Use an automatic or remotely activated extinguisher.
- A lifejacket must be worn, not just carried. In an emergency there may be no time to don it.
- Abandonment should be a last resort: the vessel is usually easier for rescuers to find than a liferaft. Only abandon ship when the vessel is clearly going to sink or is on fire and uncontrollable.
- Gas (LPG) is heavier than air — it accumulates unseen in the bilges. Always ventilate before starting the engine or using naked flames below decks.
- A mobile phone CANNOT replace VHF for distress communications — it alerts only one person and cannot be used for two-way communication with a helicopter.
