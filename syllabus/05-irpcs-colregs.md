# Topic 5 — IRPCS/COLREGS: International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea

> **SOURCE:** `research/colregs-irpcs-text.md` — USCG Navigation Center amalgamated rules, retrieved 2026-03-17. All key facts below are cited against that file.

---

## Summary

The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (IRPCS) — commonly called COLREGS — are a set of internationally agreed rules that govern how vessels must behave to avoid collisions. They are given force in UK law by the Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations 1996 (MSN 1781 M+F).

**Scope:** The IRPCS apply to all vessels on the high seas and all waters connected to the high seas that are navigable by seagoing vessels. They apply in daylight, darkness, and restricted visibility.

**Structure:** The IRPCS are organised into Parts A through F, covering 38 Rules and 4 Annexes:
- Part A (Rules 1–3): General — application, responsibility, definitions
- Part B (Rules 4–19): Steering and Sailing Rules (divided into three sections: conduct in any visibility; conduct in sight of one another; conduct in restricted visibility)
- Part C (Rules 20–31): Lights and Shapes
- Part D (Rules 32–37): Sound and Light Signals
- Part E (Rule 38): Exemptions
- Part F: Verification of compliance (added by 2013 amendment)
- Annexes I–IV: Technical specifications for lights/shapes, distress signals, etc.

**RYA Day Skipper requirement:** "Full knowledge" is required for Rules 5, 7, 8, 9, 10, and 12–19. These are the core steering and sailing rules.

**Why it matters:** Every vessel — whether a dinghy, sailing yacht, motor cruiser, or supertanker — must comply with the IRPCS whenever it is on navigable waters connected to the sea. Understanding which vessel gives way to which, how to assess a collision risk, and what actions to take is a fundamental safety skill for any skipper.

**Key concepts in plain English:**
- Every vessel must keep a proper lookout at all times, using sight and sound.
- Every vessel must proceed at a safe speed.
- If another vessel's compass bearing is not changing, a collision risk exists.
- When a collision risk exists, action must be taken early and decisively — a small, hesitant alteration of course is dangerous.
- The rules distinguish between the "give-way vessel" (which must take avoiding action) and the "stand-on vessel" (which must maintain course and speed, though it too must act if collision becomes inevitable).
- A hierarchy of vessel types determines who gives way to whom: vessels restricted in ability to manoeuvre have priority over vessels constrained by draught, which have priority over fishing vessels, which have priority over sailing vessels, which have priority over power-driven vessels.
- In narrow channels, power does not automatically give way to sail — large vessels that can only navigate safely within the channel must not be impeded by smaller craft.
- Vessels must display the correct lights at night and day shapes by day to indicate their type, size, and activity.

## Key Facts

**Rule 5 — Look-out**
Every vessel shall at all times maintain a proper look-out by sight and hearing as well as by all available means appropriate in the prevailing circumstances and conditions so as to make a full appraisal of the situation and of the risk of collision. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 5]
Practical: beware blind spots caused by sails, sprayhoods, and dodgers — station a dedicated look-out when needed. [transcript page-15]

**Rule 6 — Safe Speed**
Every vessel shall at all times proceed at a safe speed so that she can take proper and effective action to avoid collision and be stopped within a distance appropriate to the prevailing circumstances and conditions. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 6]
Factors include: visibility, traffic density, manoeuvrability, wind/sea/current, proximity of hazards, and (for radar-equipped vessels) radar range and contact density.

**Rule 7 — Risk of Collision**
Every vessel shall use all available means to determine if risk of collision exists. If there is any doubt, such risk shall be deemed to exist. Risk exists in particular when the compass bearing of an approaching vessel does not appreciably change. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 7]
Practical method: take compass bearings on the approaching vessel — if the bearing stays steady (or the vessel stays lined up against a stanchion or stay), a risk of collision exists. [transcript page-15]

**Rule 8 — Action to Avoid Collision**
Any action shall be positive, made in ample time, and with due regard to good seamanship. Any alteration of course and/or speed shall be large enough to be readily apparent to another vessel. Action shall result in passing at a safe distance; effectiveness must be checked until the other vessel is finally past and clear. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 8]

**Rule 9 — Narrow Channels**
A vessel proceeding along a narrow channel shall keep as near to the starboard outer limit of the channel as is safe and practicable. A vessel of less than 20 m or a sailing vessel shall not impede a vessel that can safely navigate only within the channel. Power does not automatically give way to sail in a narrow channel. If crossing a channel, head as nearly as practicable at right angles. Avoid anchoring in a narrow channel. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 9; transcript page-16]

**Rule 10 — Traffic Separation Schemes (TSS)**
Vessels using a TSS shall proceed in the appropriate traffic lane in the general direction of traffic flow. Normally join or leave at the ends; if joining from the side, do so at as small an angle as practicable. If obliged to cross, cross on a heading as nearly as practicable at right angles to the traffic flow. Vessels not using a TSS shall avoid it by as wide a margin as possible. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 10]

**Rule 12 — Sailing Vessels**
When two sailing vessels approach so as to involve risk of collision:
- Vessel on port tack keeps clear of vessel on starboard tack.
- When both on same tack, the windward vessel keeps clear of the leeward vessel.
- If a port-tack vessel cannot determine the other's tack, she shall keep clear.
[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 12; transcript page-16]

**Rule 13 — Overtaking**
Any vessel overtaking any other shall keep out of the way of the vessel being overtaken. A vessel is overtaking when approaching from more than 22.5° abaft the beam. The obligation to keep clear continues until finally past and clear — regardless of subsequent bearing changes. Any vessel in this sector — power or sail — must give way. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 13; transcript page-15]

**Rule 14 — Head-on Situation**
When two power-driven vessels meet on reciprocal or nearly reciprocal courses so as to involve risk of collision, each shall alter her course to starboard so that each passes on the port side of the other. When in doubt whether the situation exists, assume it does and act accordingly. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 14; transcript page-15]

**Rule 15 — Crossing Situation**
When two power-driven vessels cross so as to involve risk of collision, the vessel that has the other on her own starboard side shall keep out of the way and shall avoid crossing ahead of the other vessel. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 15; transcript page-15]

**Rule 16 — Action by Give-way Vessel**
Every vessel directed to keep out of the way shall take early and substantial action to keep well clear. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 16]

**Rule 17 — Action by Stand-on Vessel**
The stand-on vessel shall maintain her course and speed. She may however take action alone if it becomes apparent that the give-way vessel is not acting. When collision cannot be avoided by the give-way vessel alone, the stand-on vessel shall take action to best avoid collision. [SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 17; transcript page-15]

**Rule 18 — Responsibilities Between Vessels (priority order)**
Order of precedence, highest to lowest (right of way increases up the list):
1. Vessel restricted in ability to manoeuvre (laying marks, dredging, underwater work, etc.)
2. Vessel constrained by draught *(International Rules only)*
3. Vessel engaged in fishing
4. Vessel under sail
5. Power-driven vessel
*(Vessel not under command sits above all the above in the full hierarchy.)*
[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 18; transcript page-16]

**Rule 19 — Restricted Visibility**
See also: syllabus/15-restricted-visibility.md for full treatment.
- Applies to vessels not in sight of one another in or near restricted visibility.
- Every vessel shall proceed at a safe speed adapted to conditions; engines ready for immediate manoeuvre.
- If risk of collision exists from a radar contact, take avoiding action in ample time.
- Do NOT alter course to port for a vessel forward of the beam (except for a vessel being overtaken).
- On hearing a fog signal apparently forward of the beam: reduce to minimum steerage way; if necessary stop; navigate with extreme caution.
[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 19]

**Light arcs (Rule 21)**
| Light | Arc |
|-------|-----|
| Masthead / steaming light | 225° |
| Sidelight (port — red; starboard — green) | 112.5° each |
| Sternlight (white) | 135° |
| All-round light | 360° |
[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 21; transcript page-17]

**Sailing vessel lights (Rule 25)**
- Under sail (less than 20 m): may use a tricolour masthead light OR separate sidelights + sternlight.
- Tricolour must NEVER be used simultaneously with a steaming (masthead) light.
- When motoring (power or motor-sailing): display steaming light, sidelights, sternlight; and by day display a black cone apex-downwards (motoring cone).
[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 25; transcript page-17]

- Three short blasts (•••): operating astern propulsion

[SRC: colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 34; transcript page-16]

- Vessel at anchor: rapid bell ringing for ~5 seconds, every 1 minute

## ⚠️ Safety-Critical Notes

- The IRPCS apply to ALL vessels at all times on all waters navigable by seagoing vessels. Ignorance of the rules is not a defence.
- Being the "stand-on" vessel does not mean doing nothing — if the give-way vessel fails to act, the stand-on vessel must ultimately manoeuvre to avoid collision.
- Give-way action must be early and bold — a small, late alteration of course may be misread by the other vessel and make the situation worse.
- In a narrow channel, do not assume right of way over large vessels — they may have no room to manoeuvre and a collision would be catastrophic.
- A vessel overtaking another remains the give-way vessel until well past and clear — even if the other vessel appears to slow down.
- Sound signals are mandatory — five short blasts means danger or doubt; if you hear it, something is wrong.
