## Summary

The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (IRPCS) — commonly called COLREGS — are a set of internationally agreed rules that govern how vessels must behave to avoid collisions. They are given force in UK law by the Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations 1996 (MSN 1781 M+F).

**Scope:** The IRPCS apply to all vessels on the high seas and all waters connected to the high seas that are navigable by seagoing vessels. They apply in daylight, darkness, and restricted visibility.

**Structure:** The IRPCS are organised into Parts A through F, covering 38 Rules and 4 Annexes:
- Part A (Rules 1–3): General — application, responsibility, definitions
- Part B (Rules 4–19): Steering and Sailing Rules (divided into three sections: conduct in any visibility; conduct in sight of one another; conduct in restricted visibility)
- Part C (Rules 20–31): Lights and Shapes
- Part D (Rules 32–37): Sound and Light Signals
- Part E (Rule 38): Exemptions
- Part F: Verification of compliance (added by 2013 amendment)
- Annexes I–IV: Technical specifications for lights/shapes, distress signals, etc.

**RYA Day Skipper requirement:** "Full knowledge" is required for Rules 5, 7, 8, 9, 10, and 12–19. These are the core steering and sailing rules.

**Why it matters:** Every vessel — whether a dinghy, sailing yacht, motor cruiser, or supertanker — must comply with the IRPCS whenever it is on navigable waters connected to the sea. Understanding which vessel gives way to which, how to assess a collision risk, and what actions to take is a fundamental safety skill for any skipper.

**Key concepts in plain English:**
- Every vessel must keep a proper lookout at all times, using sight and sound.
- Every vessel must proceed at a safe speed.
- If another vessel's compass bearing is not changing, a collision risk exists.
- When a collision risk exists, action must be taken early and decisively — a small, hesitant alteration of course is dangerous.
- The rules distinguish between the "give-way vessel" (which must take avoiding action) and the "stand-on vessel" (which must maintain course and speed, though it too must act if collision becomes inevitable).
- A hierarchy of vessel types determines who gives way to whom: vessels restricted in ability to manoeuvre have priority over vessels constrained by draught, which have priority over fishing vessels, which have priority over sailing vessels, which have priority over power-driven vessels.
- In narrow channels, power does not automatically give way to sail — large vessels that can only navigate safely within the channel must not be impeded by smaller craft.
- Vessels must display the correct lights at night and day shapes by day to indicate their type, size, and activity.

## ⚠️ Safety-Critical Notes

- The IRPCS apply to ALL vessels at all times on all waters navigable by seagoing vessels. Ignorance of the rules is not a defence.
- Being the "stand-on" vessel does not mean doing nothing — if the give-way vessel fails to act, the stand-on vessel must ultimately manoeuvre to avoid collision.
- Give-way action must be early and bold — a small, late alteration of course may be misread by the other vessel and make the situation worse.
- In a narrow channel, do not assume right of way over large vessels — they may have no room to manoeuvre and a collision would be catastrophic.
- A vessel overtaking another remains the give-way vessel until well past and clear — even if the other vessel appears to slow down.
- Sound signals are mandatory — five short blasts means danger or doubt; if you hear it, something is wrong.

**Rule 5 — Look-out**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] Every vessel shall at all times maintain a proper look-out by sight and hearing, as well as by all available means appropriate, to make a full appraisal of the situation and of the risk of collision. Practical note (from transcript page-15): beware of blind spots caused by sails, sprayhoods, and dodgers.

**Rule 7 — Risk of collision**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] A vessel must use all available means to determine whether a risk of collision exists. If there is any doubt, risk shall be deemed to exist. Practical method (from transcript page-15): take a bearing on the approaching vessel while on a steady course — if the bearing does not change, risk of collision exists. Lining up the vessel against a stanchion or stay is a practical bearing check.

**Rule 8 — Action to avoid collision**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] Any action taken shall be positive, made in ample time, and shall result in passing at a safe distance. Alterations of course or speed shall be large enough to be readily apparent to the other vessel. A series of small alterations is dangerous. (Practical note from transcript page-15: give-way vessels must make intentions clear with an early, bold alteration of course.)

**Rule 9 — Narrow channels**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] A vessel proceeding along a narrow channel shall keep to the starboard side of the channel. A vessel shall not impede the passage of a vessel which can only safely navigate within the channel (i.e. a small vessel must not get in the way of a deep-draught ship). Practical notes from transcript page-16: power does not automatically give way to sail in a narrow channel; crossing vessels should head at 90° to the channel; small craft should sail outside the channel where possible; avoid anchoring in a narrow channel.

**Rule 10 — Traffic Separation Schemes**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] Vessels using a TSS shall proceed in the appropriate traffic lane in the direction of the traffic flow. Vessels shall join or leave the lane at the ends; if joining from the side, do so at as small an angle to the general direction as practicable. Vessels shall avoid crossing lanes, but if obliged to cross, shall do so on a heading as nearly as practicable at right angles to the traffic flow. Vessels not using a TSS shall avoid it by as wide a margin as possible.

**Rule 12 — Sailing vessels**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] When two sailing vessels are approaching one another: (a) the vessel on port tack keeps clear of the vessel on starboard tack; (b) when both on the same tack, the windward vessel keeps clear; (c) if a port-tack vessel cannot determine which tack the other vessel is on, she shall keep clear. (Practical note from transcript page-16: "Port tack always keeps clear whatever the point of sailing" and "Yachts on same tack — windward yacht keeps clear.")

**Rules 13–15 — Overtaking, Head-on, Crossing**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available]
- Rule 13 (Overtaking): Any vessel overtaking any other shall keep out of the way. A vessel is overtaking if she approaches from a direction more than 22.5° abaft the beam of the other vessel. Any vessel in this sector — power or sail — must give way. (Transcript page-15.)
- Rule 14 (Head-on): When two power-driven vessels meet on reciprocal or near-reciprocal courses, both shall alter course to starboard. (Transcript page-15.)
- Rule 15 (Crossing): When two power-driven vessels are crossing, the vessel which has the other on her starboard side shall give way and shall avoid crossing ahead. (Transcript page-15: "A is on the starboard side of B — B gives way to A.")

**Rules 16–17 — Give-way and stand-on**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available]
- Rule 16: The give-way vessel shall take early and substantial action to keep well clear.
- Rule 17: The stand-on vessel shall maintain course and speed. If it becomes apparent the give-way vessel is not acting, the stand-on vessel may take action. As soon as collision cannot be avoided by the give-way vessel alone, the stand-on vessel shall take best action to avoid collision.

**Rule 18 — Responsibilities between vessels (order of priority)**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] The order of priority from highest to lowest (as shown in transcript page-16):
1. Vessel restricted in ability to manoeuvre (e.g. dredging, laying marks)
2. Vessel constrained by draught
3. Vessel engaged in fishing
4. Vessel under sail
5. Power-driven vessel

**Rule 19 — Conduct in restricted visibility**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] See also syllabus/15-restricted-visibility.md. Every vessel shall proceed at a safe speed adapted to conditions. A vessel hearing a fog signal apparently forward of the beam shall reduce speed to minimum steerage way. A vessel detecting another vessel by radar shall take avoiding action in ample time. No alteration of course to port for a vessel forward of the beam. See Topic 15 file for fuller treatment.

**Power vs sail give-way hierarchy (general)**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] A sailing vessel under sail alone generally has right of way over a power-driven vessel, but must not impede a vessel in a narrow channel, and a vessel overtaking another is always the give-way vessel regardless of whether it is sail or power. A sailing vessel that is also motoring is treated as a power-driven vessel and must display the motoring cone by day. (Transcript page-16, page-17.)

**Lights and shapes — specific requirements**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] Light arc specifications: masthead/steaming light — 225° arc forward; sidelights — 112.5° each; sternlight — 135° (22.5° per side of stern). (Transcript page-17: degree labels visible on sector diagram as 225°, 112.5°, 112.5°, 22.5°.) Full requirements for each vessel type by Rule 20–31 require verification against SRC-002 Annexes.

**Sound signals (in sight of each other)**
[UNVERIFIED — awaiting colregs-irpcs-text.md: cite SRC-002 when available] From transcript page-16 (verify rule references against SRC-002, Rule 34):
- One short blast: I am altering course to starboard
- Two short blasts: I am altering course to port
- Three short blasts: my engines are going astern
- Five short blasts (or more): doubt/danger signal
- Two short blasts + one long blast (in fog): engines going astern
