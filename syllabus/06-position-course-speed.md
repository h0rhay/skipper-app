# Position, Course and Speed

## Summary
This topic defines the fundamental units and concepts of marine navigation: how position is expressed using latitude and longitude, how the nautical mile and the knot relate to the Earth's geometry, and the four types of bearing (true, magnetic, compass, and relative). These definitions underpin all chartwork and passage planning.

## Key Facts

### Latitude and Longitude
- **Lines of longitude** (meridians) run from the North Pole to the South Pole, dividing the Earth into segments like an orange. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- **Lines of latitude** (parallels) are obtained by projecting angles from the centre of the Earth to points on its surface; they run parallel to the Equator. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- The **Greenwich Meridian** (Prime Meridian) is 0° longitude; longitude is measured 0°–180° East or West. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- The **Equator** is 0° latitude; latitude is measured 0°–90° North or South. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Positions are written as **degrees, minutes, and decimal minutes**: e.g., 50° 08'.64N 004° 43'.20W. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Latitude is always written first, longitude second. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### The Nautical Mile and the Knot
- One **nautical mile** = 1 minute (1') of latitude. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- For all practical navigation purposes, 1 nautical mile = **1852 metres**. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- A **knot** is one nautical mile per hour — the standard unit of speed at sea. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Speed, distance, and time relate by the formula: **Distance = Speed × Time** (where time is in hours and distance in nautical miles). [Source: SRC-001]
- Distance is **always measured on the latitude scale** (the vertical scale on the sides of a chart); 1' of latitude = 1 nautical mile. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- The **longitude scale** must never be used to measure distance because the spacing of meridians varies with latitude. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### Plotting a Position by Latitude and Longitude
- Using **dividers**: set one point on the latitude scale at the required latitude, then step across to the longitude. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Step 1: mark off latitude from the side scale; Step 2: mark off longitude from the top or bottom scale. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### Bearing Types
- A **true bearing** is measured clockwise from True North (000°T to 360°T); it is the bearing used on charts. [Source: SRC-001]
- A **magnetic bearing** is measured from Magnetic North; differs from true bearing by the amount of **variation**. [Source: SRC-001]
- A **compass bearing** is what the steering compass shows; differs from magnetic bearing by the amount of **deviation** caused by the boat's own magnetic field. [Source: SRC-001]
- A **relative bearing** is measured from the vessel's bow (000° ahead, 090° to starboard, 180° astern, 270° to port). [Source: SRC-001]
- The notation **(T)** indicates a true bearing; **(M)** indicates magnetic; **(C)** indicates compass — e.g., 246°(T). [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### Course and Speed
- **Course** is the direction in which the vessel is steered, expressed as a three-figure bearing (e.g., 246°T). [Source: SRC-001]
- **Speed through the water** is the vessel's speed relative to the water; **speed over the ground** also accounts for tidal stream. [Source: SRC-001]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Latitude | Angular distance north or south of the Equator, measured in degrees and minutes | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |
| Longitude | Angular distance east or west of the Greenwich Meridian, measured in degrees and minutes | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |
| Nautical mile | Unit of distance at sea = 1 minute of latitude = 1852 m | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |
| Knot | Unit of speed = 1 nautical mile per hour | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |
| True bearing | Bearing measured clockwise from True North | SRC-001 |
| Magnetic bearing | Bearing measured from Magnetic North | SRC-001 |
| Compass bearing | Reading from the vessel's compass (includes deviation) | SRC-001 |
| Relative bearing | Bearing measured from the vessel's bow | SRC-001 |
| Variation | Difference between True North and Magnetic North at a given location | SRC-001 |
| Deviation | Error in compass reading caused by the vessel's own magnetic field | SRC-001 |
| Meridian | Line of longitude — runs pole to pole | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |
| Parallel of latitude | Line of latitude — runs parallel to the Equator | SRC-001; transcript p.20 |

## Flashcard Candidates
- Q: How many metres are in one nautical mile? | A: 1852 metres | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: What is a knot? | A: One nautical mile per hour | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: One minute of latitude equals what distance? | A: One nautical mile | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: Why must you never measure distance on the longitude scale? | A: Meridians converge towards the poles, so the spacing varies with latitude and gives incorrect distances | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: What do the letters (T), (M), and (C) mean after a bearing? | A: (T) = True, (M) = Magnetic, (C) = Compass | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: When recording a position, which is written first — latitude or longitude? | A: Latitude first, then longitude | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: What is a relative bearing? | A: A bearing measured from the vessel's bow (not from north) | Source: SRC-001 |

## MCQ Candidates

Q: What is the unit of speed used at sea?
(a) Miles per hour
(b) Knots
(c) Kilometres per hour
(d) Metres per second
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: One nautical mile is equal to how many minutes of latitude?
(a) 10
(b) 5
(c) 2
(d) 1
Correct: d
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: When measuring distance on a chart, which scale must be used?
(a) The longitude scale at the top or bottom of the chart
(b) The latitude scale on the sides of the chart
(c) Either scale — they are interchangeable
(d) A separate distance bar scale only
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: A bearing of 246°(T) means:
(a) 246° measured clockwise from Magnetic North
(b) 246° measured from the vessel's bow
(c) 246° measured clockwise from True North
(d) 246° measured anticlockwise from True North
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: A vessel is travelling at 6 knots. How far will it travel in 30 minutes?
(a) 6 nautical miles
(b) 3 nautical miles
(c) 12 nautical miles
(d) 2 nautical miles
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001
