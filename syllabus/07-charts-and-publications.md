# Navigational Charts and Publications

## Summary

Navigational charts are the fundamental tool of coastal navigation. British Admiralty (UKHO) charts use standardised symbols and abbreviations defined in NP5011 (Chart 5011). Charts represent the sea area with depth soundings referenced to Chart Datum, colour-coded zones for land, drying areas and water, and a comprehensive symbol system for hazards, aids to navigation, and navigational features. Direction and distance are measured using the latitude and longitude scales. Charts must be kept corrected using Notices to Mariners (NtM).

## Key Facts

- Chart scale is expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1:75,000 meaning 1 unit on chart = 75,000 units on earth). Larger-scale charts show more detail of a smaller area. [Source: SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18]
- The geodetic datum on which a chart is produced must match the datum set in the navigator's GPS — a mismatch can cause position errors. [Source: SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18]
- All depths on British Admiralty charts are measured below Chart Datum (CD), which approximates to Lowest Astronomical Tide (LAT). [Source: SRC-004, Section I]
- Sounding figures on metric charts are in metres and decimetres: the subscript smaller digit represents tenths of a metre (e.g., 5₄ = 5.4 m). [Source: SRC-004, Section I]
- Drying heights (areas that cover and uncover with the tide) are shown as underlined figures on a green background, representing height above Chart Datum. [Source: SRC-004, Section I; transcript page-19]
- Standard depth contours on Admiralty charts: 2 m, 5 m, 10 m, 20 m, 30 m, 50 m, 100 m, 200 m. The 5 m and 10 m contours are particularly important for coastal navigation. [Source: SRC-004, Section I]
- Colour coding: dark blue = 0–2 m, medium blue = 2–5 m, light blue = 5–10 m, white = deeper water, green = drying area, yellow/white = land above MHWS. [Source: SRC-004, Sections C, I]
- A rock that covers and uncovers (drying rock) is shown as an asterisk symbol (*); a rock awash at Chart Datum is shown as a plus sign in a circle with dotted surround (NP5011 K12). [Source: SRC-004, Section K]
- A submerged rock of unknown depth dangerous to navigation is shown as a plus sign with dotted surround (NP5011 K13). [Source: SRC-004, Section K]
- Dangerous wrecks are shown with a dotted (danger) line surround; non-dangerous wrecks have no dotted surround. [Source: SRC-004, Section K]
- A full light description format is: Characteristic Colour Period Elevation Range — e.g., Fl(3) R 10s 13m 5M. Elevation is above MHWS. [Source: SRC-004, Section P]
- A tidal diamond (magenta ◇ with letter) on the chart references a table in the title block giving tidal stream direction and rate (springs and neaps) for each hour relative to HW at a reference port. [Source: SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-18]
- Tidal stream arrows on charts show flood/ebb direction; numbers alongside (e.g., 14,28) indicate mean spring and neap rates in tenths of a knot. [Source: SRC-004, Section H]
- A compass rose on the chart gives the local magnetic variation and its annual rate of change. [Source: SRC-004, Section B; transcript page-18]
- Latitude scale (vertical margins) is used for measuring distance: 1 minute of latitude = 1 nautical mile. The longitude scale (horizontal margins) must never be used for measuring distance. [Source: SRC-004, Section B; transcript page-20]
- Chart corrections are recorded in the title block and promulgated through Notices to Mariners (NtM) published by the UKHO. Block corrections (new editions/new prints) replace multiple individual corrections. [Source: SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18]
- A key to all chart symbols and abbreviations is found in NP5011 Chart 5011, published by the UKHO. [Source: SRC-004; transcript page-19]
- Anchorages are shown by an anchor symbol (⚓); anchoring-prohibited areas show the anchor symbol with a diagonal cross or prohibition circle, often over submarine cables. [Source: SRC-004, Section N]
- Traffic Separation Schemes (TSS) are shown with magenta lane arrows and a separation zone between lanes. [Source: SRC-004, Section M]
- Leading lines (two charted marks in transit) guide vessels along a safe track and are shown as a line through the two marks. [Source: SRC-004, Section M]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Chart Datum (CD) | The datum to which all charted depths and drying heights are referred; approximates to Lowest Astronomical Tide (LAT) | SRC-004, Section I |
| Sounding | A depth measurement shown on the chart in metres and decimetres, measured below Chart Datum | SRC-004, Section I |
| Drying height | The height above Chart Datum of a feature that covers at high water and uncovers at low water; shown as an underlined figure | SRC-004, Section I |
| Rock awash | A rock whose highest point is at or very near Chart Datum (always at the waterline at lowest tide); shown with K12 symbol | SRC-004, Section K |
| Wreck | A sunken or stranded vessel; dangerous wrecks are enclosed in a dotted danger line on the chart | SRC-004, Section K |
| Leading line | A line created by two charted marks in transit, indicating a safe track into harbour or through a channel | SRC-004, Section M |
| Transit | Two charted objects observed in line from the vessel; provides a precise position line or track | SRC-004, Section M; transcript page-27 |
| Geodetic datum | The mathematical model of the earth's shape on which a chart's coordinate system is based; must match GPS settings | SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18 |
| Isobar | A line on a synoptic chart connecting points of equal atmospheric pressure (not a chart symbol per se, but appears on meteorological charts) | SRC-001 |
| NtM | Notice to Mariners — official publication of chart corrections issued by the UKHO | SRC-004, Section A |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What does an underlined sounding figure on a chart indicate? | A: A drying height — the feature is above Chart Datum and dries at low water; the figure is the height above CD in metres. | Source: SRC-004, Section I
- Q: What is the difference between the K12 and K13 rock symbols? | A: K12 (plus in circle with dots) = rock awash at Chart Datum — always at or very near the surface. K13 (plus with dots, no circle) = submerged rock, depth unknown — always underwater but potentially shallow. | Source: SRC-004, Section K
- Q: What format is used for a light description on an Admiralty chart? | A: Characteristic, Colour, Period, Elevation, Range — e.g., Fl(3) R 10s 13m 5M. | Source: SRC-004, Section P
- Q: Why must you never use the longitude scale to measure distance on a chart? | A: Because lines of longitude converge toward the poles — the spacing in millimetres varies with latitude. Only the latitude scale (1' = 1 nautical mile) gives consistent distance measurement. | Source: SRC-004, Section B; transcript page-20
- Q: What does a magenta diamond with a letter (◇A) on a chart represent? | A: A tidal diamond — it refers to a table in the chart's title block giving tidal stream direction and rate for each hour relative to HW at a standard port. | Source: SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-18
- Q: How are chart corrections promulgated and recorded? | A: Through Notices to Mariners (NtM) issued by the UKHO; correction dates are recorded in the chart's title block. Block corrections are applied when a new edition or new print is issued. | Source: SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18

## Safety-Critical Notes

- A wreck symbol enclosed in a dotted (danger) line means the wreck is a hazard to surface navigation. Do not attempt to pass over without confirmed, current depth information. [SRC-004, Section K]
- Rocks marked K13 (submerged, depth unknown) must be treated as potentially dangerous regardless of the charted depth in the vicinity. Always give such symbols a generous clearance. [SRC-004, Section K]
- Depth qualifiers ED (Existence Doubtful), SD (Sounding Doubtful), Rep (Reported), and PA (Position Approximate) indicate reduced survey reliability — treat these areas with extra caution. [SRC-004, Section I]
- The geodetic datum on the chart must match the GPS datum setting. A mismatch can displace a plotted position by tens or hundreds of metres — enough to place a vessel on a charted hazard. [SRC-004, Section A; transcript page-18]
