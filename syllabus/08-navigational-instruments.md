# Navigational Drawing Instruments

## Summary
This topic covers the instruments used at the chart table for plotting positions, transferring bearings, and measuring distance on a nautical chart. The principal tools are parallel rulers (walking or rolling), dividers, and proprietary plotters (such as the Portland or Breton plotter). Competence with these tools is assessed in the open-book chartwork paper of the Day Skipper exam.

## Key Facts

### Parallel Rulers
- **Parallel rulers** consist of two straight edges linked so that they remain parallel as one walks across the chart; used to transfer a bearing from a compass rose to a point on the chart, or vice versa. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- To transfer a bearing using parallel rulers: (1) align one edge with the centre of the compass rose and the required bearing; (2) hold that rule steady and "walk" the other rule across the chart; (3) when aligned with the required point, draw a line along the edge in the direction of the bearing. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- **Rolling parallel rulers** operate on a rolling cylinder rather than linked edges; same principle. [Source: SRC-001]
- The main risk with parallel rulers is losing the alignment ("slipping") when walking them across the chart. [Source: SRC-001]

### Proprietary Plotters (Portland/Breton Plotter)
- A **Portland plotter** (or Breton plotter) combines a transparent protractor grid with a straight edge, eliminating the need to use the compass rose. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- To plot a bearing with a plotter: (1) dial in (rotate the grid to) the required bearing; (2) align the edge of the plotter with the starting point; (3) line up the plotter's grid with the chart's latitude and longitude lines; (4) draw a line parallel to the plotter's edge in the direction of the bearing. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Plotters are faster to use than parallel rulers once mastered, and do not require access to a compass rose. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### Dividers
- **Dividers** are a two-legged instrument used to measure distances on a chart and to pick off latitudes or longitudes. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- To measure distance: set dividers to the required distance on the **latitude scale** (side of the chart) at the same latitude as the position; never measure on the longitude scale. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Dividers are also used to step off equal distances along a course line (e.g., to check estimated position at hourly intervals). [Source: SRC-001]
- Always measure distance at the **latitude-scale level with your position** — the latitude scale stretches very slightly with the Mercator projection, so accuracy requires using the scale at the relevant latitude. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

### Chart Table Equipment
- A complete chart table kit typically includes: parallel rulers or plotter, dividers, pencils (2B or similar), eraser, soft cloth (to avoid chart damage). [Source: SRC-001]
- **Pencil** is always used for chartwork — never pen — so workings can be erased. [Source: SRC-001]

### Plotting a Position by Distance and Bearing
- Example: position given as "246°(T) from Skerries Point 1.6M". [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Using a plotter: dial in 246°(T), align with Skerries Point, align grid to chart latitude/longitude lines, draw the line, then measure 1.6M from Skerries Point along that line. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]
- Using parallel rulers: line up one edge with the compass rose centre and 246°(T), walk rules to Skerries Point, draw the bearing line, then measure 1.6M along it. [Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20]

## Flashcard Candidates
- Q: What are parallel rulers used for in chartwork? | A: Transferring a bearing from the compass rose to a point on the chart, or from a feature to the compass rose | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: Why must you never use the longitude scale to measure distance? | A: Meridians converge towards the poles, making longitude spacing vary with latitude and giving incorrect distances | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: What advantage does a Portland/Breton plotter have over parallel rulers? | A: It does not require access to the compass rose — the protractor grid is built in | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
- Q: What is always used for chartwork — pencil or pen? | A: Pencil — so workings can be erased | Source: SRC-001
- Q: When using dividers to measure distance, at which level should you read the latitude scale? | A: At the same latitude as your position | Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

## MCQ Candidates

Q: What is the primary purpose of dividers in chart navigation?
(a) Transferring bearings from the compass rose
(b) Measuring distances on the chart
(c) Drawing course lines
(d) Reading chart datum levels
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: When using parallel rulers to transfer a bearing, what is the main practical risk?
(a) Measuring on the longitude scale
(b) The rulers slipping and losing alignment while walking across the chart
(c) Setting the wrong bearing on the compass rose
(d) Drawing the line in the wrong direction
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: A plotter is set to 246°(T) and aligned with a chart feature. What must be done next before drawing the bearing line?
(a) Transfer the plotter to the compass rose
(b) Line up the plotter's grid with the chart's latitude and longitude lines
(c) Set the dividers to the bearing distance
(d) Walk the plotter to the nearest depth contour
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20

Q: Which scale on a nautical chart must be used to measure distance with dividers?
(a) The longitude scale
(b) The depth scale
(c) The latitude scale on the sides of the chart
(d) The distance bar scale at the chart's margin
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript p.20
