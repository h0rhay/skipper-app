# Compass

## Summary

The compass is the primary instrument for steering and taking bearings at sea. Navigators must understand three north references — True North (geographic), Magnetic North (compass rose), and Compass North (the ship's compass reading) — and be able to convert between them. Variation (True vs Magnetic) changes with position and time. Deviation (Magnetic vs Compass) changes with the vessel's heading and is caused by onboard magnetic influences. Chartwork is always in True; compass courses must be converted using both variation and deviation. A hand-bearing compass is used to take bearings on charted objects for position fixing and deviation checking.

## Key Facts

- Charts show direction as True (°T), referenced to True (geographic) North. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- A compass points to Magnetic North, not True North. The angular difference between True North and Magnetic North at a given position is called variation. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Variation is expressed as East or West: if variation is West, magnetic bearing is greater than true bearing; if East, magnetic bearing is smaller. Rule: West is best (magnetic > true), East is least. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Example: with 7°W variation — True 060°(T) = Magnetic 067°(M). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Variation changes with geographic position and changes slowly over time. Its value and annual rate of change are printed on the compass rose of the relevant chart. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Deviation is caused by ferrous metals and electromagnetic fields onboard (engine, speakers, electronics, mobile phones, enamel mugs near compass). It affects the ship's compass and varies with the vessel's heading. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- A compass is "swung" to determine deviation on each heading; results are recorded in a deviation card or curve showing deviation for each heading of the ship. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- To convert True to Compass: apply variation (+ W, − E), then apply deviation (+ W, − E). To convert Compass to True: reverse the process (− W, + E, then − W, + E). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Worked example: True 060°(T) + 7°W variation = 067°(M) − 2°E deviation = 065°(C). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]
- Compass error = variation + deviation combined; it is the total difference between True North and the compass reading. [Source: SRC-001]
- A hand-bearing compass is used on deck (away from the steering compass) to take accurate bearings on charted objects for visual position fixing. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- Deviation can be checked by pointing the boat directly at a transit and comparing the compass reading with the known magnetic bearing of the transit. Any difference is the deviation on that heading. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| True North | The direction of the geographic North Pole; the reference for chart directions | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |
| Magnetic North | The direction toward the Earth's magnetic north pole; the reference to which a compass needle points | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |
| Compass North | The direction indicated by the ship's compass on a given heading, after the effects of onboard magnetic influences (deviation) | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |
| Variation | The angular difference between True North and Magnetic North at a given position; expressed as East or West | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |
| Deviation | The angular difference between Magnetic North and Compass North on a given vessel heading, caused by onboard magnetic influences | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |
| Compass error | The combined total of variation and deviation; the total angular difference between True North and the compass reading | SRC-001 |
| Deviation card | A table or curve listing the deviation of the ship's compass for each heading, produced by swinging the compass | SRC-001; transcript page-21 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What is variation? | A: The angular difference between True North and Magnetic North at a given position. It is printed on the chart compass rose and changes slowly over time. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21
- Q: What is deviation? | A: The angular difference between Magnetic North and Compass North, caused by ferrous metals and electromagnetic fields onboard. It varies with the vessel's heading. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21
- Q: If variation is 5°W and you have a true bearing of 080°(T), what is the magnetic bearing? | A: 085°(M). West variation means magnetic is greater — add 5°. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21
- Q: How do you convert a True bearing to a Compass course? | A: Apply variation: True + W variation (or − E) = Magnetic. Then apply deviation: Magnetic + W deviation (or − E) = Compass. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21
- Q: Where do you find the value of variation for your position? | A: On the compass rose of the relevant chart. It also shows the annual rate of change so you can apply an update for the current year. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21

## MCQ Candidates

Q: A chart shows a compass rose with variation 7°W. You have plotted a True course of 055°(T). What is the equivalent Magnetic course?
(a) 048°(M)
(b) 062°(M)
(c) 055°(M)
(d) 070°(M)
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21

Q: What causes deviation in a ship's compass?
(a) The difference between True North and Magnetic North
(b) Onboard ferrous metals and electromagnetic fields
(c) The annual change in the Earth's magnetic field
(d) Incorrect chart datum settings
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21

Q: The ship's compass reads 070°(C). The transit you are pointed at bears 068°(M). What is the deviation on this heading?
(a) 2°E
(b) 2°W
(c) 138°E
(d) 0° (no deviation)
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21

Q: Chartwork is carried out in which reference system?
(a) Compass (°C)
(b) Magnetic (°M)
(c) True (°T)
(d) Grid (°G)
Correct: (c)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-21
