# Chartwork

## Summary

Chartwork is the practical application of navigation on the chart. It encompasses plotting positions (by latitude/longitude, by bearing and distance, or by GPS), tracking the vessel's progress using dead reckoning and estimated position, fixing position visually using cross bearings and transits, and planning a course to steer that accounts for tidal stream and leeway. GPS and waypoints are used to supplement — but not replace — traditional chartwork.

## Key Facts

- A nautical mile is 1,852 metres; 1 minute of latitude = 1 nautical mile. Speed is measured in knots (1 knot = 1 nautical mile per hour). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-20]
- Distance on the chart is measured on the latitude scale on the side margins, always at the same latitude as the position being measured. The longitude scale must never be used for measuring distance. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-20]
- A position can be plotted by latitude and longitude (using dividers against the latitude and longitude scales), or by bearing and distance from a charted object. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-20]
- Dead Reckoning (DR): an approximate position calculated from a known starting point using the course steered and the distance run (from the ship's log), without allowing for tide or leeway. DR position is marked on the chart with a triangle symbol. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25]
- Leeway is the angular difference between the direction the bow is pointing (course steered) and the direction the vessel is actually moving through the water (water track), caused by wind pressure on the hull and sails. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25]
- Estimated Position (EP): a position calculated from a DR position by additionally applying leeway to get the water track, then applying the tidal vector (set and rate for the elapsed time). EP is marked on the chart with a half-triangle (◁) symbol. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25]
- Chartwork symbols: triangle (△) = DR position; half-triangle (◁) = EP; circle (○) = fix; circle with cross (⊕) = waypoint; dashed line = water track; solid line = ground track; double arrow = tidal set and drift. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25]
- To plot an EP: (1) plot water track from DR position for distance run, (2) apply leeway to get water track direction, (3) add tidal vector (set and drift), (4) mark the EP. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25]
- A visual fix is obtained by taking simultaneous bearings on two or three charted objects and drawing the bearing lines on the chart; the intersection is the fix. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- Bearings rarely form a perfect intersection — three lines typically form a small triangle called a "cocked hat". The vessel's position is within the cocked hat; place it toward the danger. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- For best fix accuracy: use three objects if possible; select objects that give wide angles of cut (ideally ~60° or ~90° apart); use closer objects. Avoid objects whose bearings are too close together. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- A transit (two charted objects in line) provides a very accurate position line. Combined with a bearing on a third object at ~90°, it gives an accurate fix. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- GPS provides latitude and longitude accurate to approximately 15 metres by triangulating from orbiting satellites. It also provides Course Over Ground (COG) and Speed Over Ground (SOG). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- GPS must be backed up with other position-fixing methods (bearings, depth, visual transits) — GPS can fail due to power/aerial failure, interference, or satellite system issues. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- Waypoints (WPTs) are positions stored in the GPS memory. Always check the distance and bearing shown by the GPS against what is plotted on the chart — any discrepancy likely means an input error. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- Plot WPTs adjacent to hazards, not on them. Never input a WPT directly from a book — always plot it on the chart first. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- GPS does not automatically allow for tidal set and drift — if you steer the bearing the GPS gives to a waypoint in a cross-tide, you will sail a longer route and may be set into danger. Always pre-calculate a course to steer that accounts for the tide. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- Course to Steer (CTS): calculated graphically on the chart by: (1) plotting the intended ground track from start to destination, (2) plotting the tidal vector from the start position, (3) setting dividers to boat speed and swinging an arc from the end of the tidal vector to cross the ground track, (4) the bearing of the line from the end of the tidal vector to the crossing point is the water track — i.e., the CTS. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-26]
- A position should be recorded at regular intervals on the chart and in the ship's log. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| DR (Dead Reckoning) | An approximate position calculated from course steered and distance run, without correction for tide or leeway | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| EP (Estimated Position) | A position derived from a DR position by additionally applying leeway (water track) and tidal set and drift | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Fix | A position determined by external observations (bearings, GPS, transit) rather than calculation | SRC-001; transcript page-27 |
| COG (Course Over Ground) | The actual direction of travel of the vessel across the seabed, accounting for tide and leeway | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| SOG (Speed Over Ground) | The actual speed of the vessel over the seabed, accounting for tidal stream | SRC-001; transcript page-27 |
| CTS (Course to Steer) | The compass course to steer (water track) that, when combined with the tidal stream, results in the desired ground track | SRC-001; transcript page-26 |
| Leeway | The angular difference between the course steered (direction the bow points) and the water track (direction through the water), caused by wind | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Set | The direction in which the tidal stream flows (in degrees True) | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Drift | The distance a vessel is carried by the tidal stream in a given time (or the rate in knots) | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Water track | The direction and distance the vessel moves through the water, after allowing for leeway but before applying tidal stream | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Ground track | The actual path of the vessel over the seabed, after the combined effects of course steered, leeway, and tidal stream | SRC-001; transcript page-25 |
| Waypoint (WPT) | A position stored in the GPS memory for navigation reference | SRC-001; transcript page-28 |
| Cocked hat | The small triangle formed when three position lines from a visual fix do not meet at a single point | SRC-001; transcript page-27 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What is the difference between a DR position and an EP? | A: A DR position uses only course steered and distance run. An EP additionally applies leeway (to get water track) and the tidal vector — making it a better approximation of actual position. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25
- Q: What chartwork symbol is used for (a) a DR position, (b) an EP, and (c) a fix? | A: (a) Triangle △; (b) half-triangle (open triangle) ◁; (c) circle ○. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25
- Q: How do you construct a Course to Steer graphically on the chart? | A: Plot ground track from A to B. From A, plot tidal vector. From the end of the tidal vector, swing an arc equal to boat speed to cut the ground track. The bearing of the line from tidal vector end to the cutting point is the water track = CTS. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-26
- Q: What is a cocked hat and where do you place your position within it? | A: The small triangle formed when three bearing lines in a visual fix do not intersect at a single point. Place your estimated position at the corner of the cocked hat closest to any nearby hazard. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27
- Q: Why must GPS be backed up with other position information? | A: GPS can fail (power/aerial failure, interference, satellite issues) and gives no warning when it provides an incorrect position. An independent backup prevents navigational error. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27
- Q: What is the danger of steering the bearing that a GPS gives you directly to a waypoint when there is cross-tide? | A: The tidal stream will push you off the straight-line track — you will sail a longer route and may be set toward a hazard. Always calculate a course to steer that accounts for the tide. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28

## MCQ Candidates

Q: A vessel steers 070°(T) for 1 hour at 5 knots and the log records 5 miles. The tidal stream is 120°(T) at 1.5 knots. What is the vessel's approximate position?
(a) 5 miles on 070°(T) from start
(b) 5 miles on 120°(T) from start
(c) The resultant of 5 miles on 070°(T) plus 1.5 miles on 120°(T)
(d) 6.5 miles on 070°(T) from start
Correct: (c)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25

Q: What does the symbol ◁ (half-triangle) represent on a chart?
(a) A fix
(b) A waypoint
(c) A dead reckoning position
(d) An estimated position
Correct: (d)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-25

Q: Three bearing lines on a chart form a small triangle. Where should you assume your position to be?
(a) At the centroid (centre) of the triangle
(b) At the corner of the triangle closest to the nearest hazard
(c) At the corner of the triangle farthest from land
(d) At the point where two of the three lines cross
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27

Q: When using a transit as a position line, what is the ideal angle for the second bearing to intersect the transit?
(a) 10–20°
(b) 30–45°
(c) Approximately 90°
(d) 180° (back bearing)
Correct: (c)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27

Q: You input a waypoint into your GPS from the chart. The GPS shows BRG 010° DIST 6.7M. You plot the waypoint on the chart and measure the bearing as 012° at 6.9M. What does this discrepancy suggest?
(a) The GPS is broken
(b) You probably entered the wrong latitude or longitude
(c) Tidal stream is affecting the display
(d) The chart datum is incorrect
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28
