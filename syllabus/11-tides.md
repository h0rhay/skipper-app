# Tides and Tidal Streams

## Summary

Tides are the regular rise and fall of sea level caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon and Sun. Understanding tidal heights is essential for calculating the depth of water over shoals, under bridges, and at harbour entrances. Tidal streams (the horizontal flow of water driven by tidal forces) affect a vessel's course, speed, and passage planning. Data is obtained from tide tables, tidal diamonds on charts, and tidal stream atlases. Secondary ports require corrections applied to the nearest standard port.

## Key Facts

- Tides are caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun. The Moon orbits the Earth over a 28-day cycle, producing two tidal cycles per day. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- Spring tides occur when the Moon and Sun are aligned (new and full moon); they produce the greatest tidal range (highest high waters and lowest low waters). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- Neap tides occur when the Moon and Sun are at right angles; they produce the smallest tidal range. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- There are two high waters (HW) and two low waters (LW) per day. The period between HW and LW is approximately 6 hours. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- Tidal range = HW height minus LW height. Compare the range with the mean range printed on the tidal curve to determine whether conditions are closer to springs or neaps. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23]
- Chart Datum (CD) is the reference level to which all charted depths and drying heights are referenced; it approximates to Lowest Astronomical Tide (LAT). [Source: SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22]
- Height of tide is measured above Chart Datum. Tide tables give the times and heights of HW and LW at standard ports for each day of the year. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- Depth of water = charted depth + height of tide (for depths below CD). For drying heights: depth of water = height of tide minus drying height (positive result = water over the feature). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- The rule of twelfths: tidal height changes approximately 1/12, 2/12, 3/12, 3/12, 2/12, 1/12 of the range in each successive hour from LW to HW (or HW to LW). The tide rises/falls fastest in the middle two hours. [Source: SRC-001]
- Standard ports have full tide tables published for each day of the year. Secondary ports do not — their tidal data is obtained by applying time and height differences (from an almanac) to the nearest standard port. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23]
- Secondary port corrections: time difference (e.g., HW +35 min) and height difference (e.g., MHWS −1.6 m) are applied to the standard port values. Interpolation is required when the standard port HW time falls between the tabulated reference times. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23]
- Tidal curves (printed for each standard port) allow the height of tide to be determined at any time between HW and LW — not just at the tabulated HW/LW times. Use the solid curve for springs and the dotted curve for neaps. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23]
- A tidal diamond (◇ with a letter) on the chart references a table in the chart's title block giving the direction (°T) and rate (knots) of the tidal stream for each hour before and after HW at a reference port, for both springs and neaps. [Source: SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-24]
- Tidal stream atlases are published books of chartlets (one per hour relative to HW Dover or other reference port) showing tidal stream direction and rates with arrows annotated with spring and neap rates. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-24]
- Tidal stream arrow numbers on charts and atlases are in tenths of a knot: e.g., 14,28 means spring rate 1.4 kn, neap rate 2.8 kn. [Source: SRC-004, Section H]
- The direction and rate of tidal streams depends on: location; whether conditions are springs, neaps, or in between; and the time relative to HW at the reference port. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-24]
- The rate between springs and neaps can be interpolated: intermediate_rate = neap_rate + (spring_rate − neap_rate) × (actual_range − neap_range) / (spring_range − neap_range). [Source: SRC-001]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Chart Datum (CD) | The datum below which depths are charted; approximates to Lowest Astronomical Tide | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22 |
| LAT | Lowest Astronomical Tide — the lowest predicted tidal level under average meteorological conditions | SRC-004, Section H |
| HAT | Highest Astronomical Tide — the highest predicted tidal level under average meteorological conditions | SRC-004, Section H |
| MHWS | Mean High Water Springs — the average height of all spring high waters | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22 |
| MHWN | Mean High Water Neaps — the average height of all neap high waters | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22 |
| MLWN | Mean Low Water Neaps — the average height of all neap low waters | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22 |
| MLWS | Mean Low Water Springs — the average height of all spring low waters | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-22 |
| HW | High Water — the highest level reached during a tidal cycle | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |
| LW | Low Water — the lowest level reached during a tidal cycle | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |
| Range | The difference between HW height and LW height for a given tidal cycle | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |
| Spring tide | A tide with a greater-than-average range, occurring near new and full moon | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |
| Neap tide | A tide with a smaller-than-average range, occurring near first and third quarter moon | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |
| Tidal diamond | A magenta diamond symbol (◇ + letter) on the chart referring to a tidal stream data table in the chart's title block | SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-24 |
| Set | The direction in which the tidal stream is flowing (°True) | SRC-001; transcript page-24 |
| Rate | The speed of the tidal stream in knots | SRC-001; transcript page-24 |
| Height of tide | The vertical distance of the water surface above Chart Datum at any given time | SRC-001; transcript page-22 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What is the rule of twelfths? | A: The tide rises or falls approximately 1/12, 2/12, 3/12, 3/12, 2/12, 1/12 of the total range in each successive hour from LW to HW. The tide moves fastest in the middle two hours. | Source: SRC-001
- Q: How do you calculate the depth of water over a charted shoal at a given time? | A: Depth of water = charted depth + height of tide. (For a drying feature: depth of water = height of tide − drying height.) | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22
- Q: What is the difference between a standard port and a secondary port? | A: A standard port has full daily tide tables. A secondary port does not — you apply time and height differences (from an almanac) to the nearest standard port to find its tidal information. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23
- Q: How do you determine whether conditions are springs or neaps? | A: Calculate the range (HW height − LW height) and compare with the mean spring range and mean neap range printed on the tidal curve for that port. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23
- Q: What information does a tidal diamond table give you? | A: For each hour before and after HW at a reference port: the direction (°T) and rate (knots) of the tidal stream at that position, for both spring and neap conditions. | Source: SRC-004, Section H; transcript page-24
- Q: What is the reference datum for light elevation on a chart, and how does it differ from Chart Datum? | A: Light elevation is given above MHWS (Mean High Water Springs). Chart Datum is approximately LAT — well below MHWS. They are different references and must not be confused. | Source: SRC-004, Section P; transcript page-22

## MCQ Candidates

Q: At a given time the height of tide is 3.2 m. The chart shows a drying height of 1.4 m for a rock. Is the rock covered or uncovered, and by how much water?
(a) Uncovered — the rock dries 1.4 m above the surface
(b) Covered by 1.8 m of water
(c) Covered by 4.6 m of water
(d) Covered by 1.4 m of water
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-22

Q: When should you use the solid curve (rather than the dotted curve) on a tidal curve diagram?
(a) For neap tides
(b) For spring tides
(c) When the range is less than the mean range
(d) At all times unless the port is a secondary port
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23

Q: A tidal stream arrow on the chart shows the figures 14,28. What do these numbers represent?
(a) Direction 14°, range 28 miles
(b) Spring rate 1.4 kn, neap rate 2.8 kn
(c) Spring rate 14 kn, neap rate 28 kn
(d) HW −14 hours, HW +28 minutes
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-004, Section H

Q: You need to find the height of tide at 0820 at Plymouth. Which instrument / publication would you use?
(a) The compass rose on the chart
(b) A tidal stream atlas
(c) The Plymouth tidal curve, using the day's HW and LW heights from the tide tables
(d) The tidal diamond table on the chart
Correct: (c)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23

Q: HW at a standard port is 5.6 m, LW is 0.8 m. The mean spring range for that port is 4.8 m and mean neap range is 2.4 m. Is today's tide closer to springs or neaps?
(a) Neaps — the range of 4.8 m equals the mean spring range exactly
(b) Springs — the range of 4.8 m matches the mean spring range
(c) Neaps — the range of 4.8 m is midway between the two
(d) Cannot be determined without checking the moon phase
Correct: (b)
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-23

## Safety-Critical Notes

- Tidal height calculations directly determine whether there is sufficient water under the keel. An error of even 0.5 m can result in grounding on a shallow bar, a drying rock, or in the approach to a drying harbour. Always verify calculations and allow an adequate safety margin. [SRC-001; transcript page-22]
- Heights of lights on charts are above MHWS — not Chart Datum. Do not use light elevation as a guide to clearance under bridges or overhead cables; refer to the charted clearance height (which is above MHWS or HAT, as specified). [SRC-004, Section P; transcript page-22]
- When calculating secondary port heights, interpolation between tabulated differences is required when the standard port HW time falls between reference times. Failure to interpolate correctly will produce an incorrect height of tide. [SRC-001; transcript page-23]
- Tidal stream strength affects all aspects of passage planning: failing to allow for a foul stream can leave a vessel short of a destination at nightfall or in deteriorating weather, or can set it onto a lee shore or into a hazardous area. [SRC-001; transcript page-24]
