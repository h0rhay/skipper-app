# Visual Aids to Navigation — Lighthouse and Beacon Light Characteristics, IALA Buoyage System

## Summary

The IALA Maritime Buoyage System divides the world into two regions. Region A covers Europe, Africa, Russia, India, Australia, and New Zealand; Region B covers the Americas, Japan, South Korea, the Philippines, and Canada. The critical difference is the colour of lateral marks: in Region A red marks are kept to port when entering harbour; in Region B this is reversed. Cardinal marks, isolated danger marks, safe water marks, special marks, and emergency wreck marks are identical in both regions.

Lighthouse and beacon lights are described by their characteristic (rhythm and colour), period (seconds per complete cycle), height above MHWS, and nominal range in nautical miles. Sector lights show different colours in different arcs to guide vessels into safe water. Leading lights, when vertically aligned, indicate a vessel is on the correct approach track.

## Key Facts

### IALA System — Overview

- Two IALA buoyage regions exist worldwide: Region A and Region B. The primary difference is the colour allocation of lateral marks. [Source: SRC-003, Overview]
- IALA Region A applies to: Europe, Africa, Russia, India, Australia, and New Zealand. [Source: SRC-003, Overview]
- IALA Region B applies to: the Americas (USA, South America, Canada, Caribbean), Japan, South Korea, the Philippines, and South East Asia. [Source: SRC-003, Overview]
- The "direction of buoyage" (conventional direction) is generally from seaward into harbour, or in the direction of flood tide. [Source: SRC-003, Overview]

### Lateral Marks — Region A

- **Port-hand lateral mark (Region A):** Colour red; shape can (cylindrical), pillar, or spar; topmark single red can; light red, any rhythm except Fl(2+1). [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]
- **Starboard-hand lateral mark (Region A):** Colour green; shape conical (nun), pillar, or spar; topmark single green cone point upward; light green, any rhythm except Fl(2+1). [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]
- When entering harbour or proceeding upstream in Region A, red marks are kept to port (left) and green marks to starboard (right). [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]
- ⚠️ IALA Region B note: In Region B the lateral mark colours are reversed — red marks to starboard, green marks to port when entering harbour. A vessel navigating from Region A to Region B waters must treat all lateral marks as reversed.

### Preferred Channel (Junction) Marks

- Preferred channel marks are used where a channel divides (bifurcation). The composite group flashing light Fl(2+1) is unique to these marks and distinguishes them from plain lateral marks. [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]
- **Preferred channel to starboard:** Body colour red with one broad green horizontal band; topmark single red can; light Fl(2+1) R. [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]
- **Preferred channel to port:** Body colour green with one broad red horizontal band; topmark single green cone point upward; light Fl(2+1) G. [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks]

### Cardinal Marks (Common to IALA A and B)

- Cardinal marks warn of danger; pass on the named side (e.g. a North cardinal: pass to the north of it, where deepest water lies). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- All cardinal marks: body black and yellow horizontal bands; pillar or spar; topmark two black cones; light white, either Q (quick, ~50–79 fl/min) or VQ (very quick, ~80–159 fl/min). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- **North cardinal:** Black over yellow (top to bottom); topmark both cones pointing upward; light Q or VQ (continuous quick flashing). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- **South cardinal:** Yellow over black (top to bottom); topmark both cones pointing downward; light Q(6)+LFl or VQ(6)+LFl (six quick flashes then one long flash). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- **East cardinal:** Black / yellow / black banding (top to bottom); topmark cones pointing away from each other (base to base); light Q(3) or VQ(3) (three quick flashes). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- **West cardinal:** Yellow / black / yellow banding (top to bottom); topmark cones pointing toward each other (point to point, hourglass); light Q(9) or VQ(9) (nine quick flashes). [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]
- Memory aid — clock face: North = continuous (12 o'clock); East = 3 flashes (3 o'clock); South = 6+LFl (6 o'clock); West = 9 flashes (9 o'clock). The long flash on the South cardinal distinguishes it from the West. [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks]

### Isolated Danger Mark

- Marks a danger with navigable water all around it; do not approach the mark. [Source: SRC-003, Isolated Danger Mark]
- Colour: black with one or more broad red horizontal bands; shape: pillar or spar; topmark: two black spheres (one above the other — mandatory); light: Fl(2) white. [Source: SRC-003, Isolated Danger Mark]

### Safe Water Mark

- Indicates safe, navigable water all around — used as landfall buoys, fairway buoys, or mid-channel marks. [Source: SRC-003, Safe Water Mark]
- Colour: red and white vertical stripes; shape: spherical (preferred), or pillar/spar with spherical topmark; topmark: single red sphere; light: white — Iso, Oc, LFl 10s, or Mo(A). [Source: SRC-003, Safe Water Mark]
- The safe water mark is the only mark that uses Morse Code A (Mo(A)) or a long flash every 10 seconds (LFl 10s). [Source: SRC-003, Safe Water Mark]

### Special Mark

- Not a navigational mark — indicates a special area or feature (e.g. ODAS buoy, cable area, recreation zone, military exercise area, TSS boundary, renewable energy installation). Refer to chart or pilot book for purpose. [Source: SRC-003, Special Mark]
- Colour: yellow; shape: any shape not conflicting with a navigational mark; topmark: yellow X (St Andrew's cross); light: yellow, any rhythm that does not conflict with white navigational lights (often Fl Y). [Source: SRC-003, Special Mark]

### Emergency Wreck Marking Buoy

- Deployed as a rapid response to mark a newly discovered, uncharted wreck; remains in position until the wreck is charted and permanent marking established. [Source: SRC-003, Emergency Wreck Marking Buoy]
- Colour: blue and yellow vertical stripes (equal bands); shape: pillar or spar; topmark: upright yellow cross (unique identifier). [Source: SRC-003, Emergency Wreck Marking Buoy]
- When multiple buoys mark the same wreck, their lights are synchronised. [Source: SRC-003, Emergency Wreck Marking Buoy]

### Lighthouse and Beacon Light Characteristics

- A light characteristic is described by its rhythm, colour, period (seconds), height above MHWS, and nominal range in nautical miles. Example: Fl(4)15s37m28M = group flashing (4 flashes), 15-second period, 37 m above MHWS, visible 28 nautical miles. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics; Transcript p.31]
- **Fixed (F):** Continuous steady light; does not flash. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Flashing (Fl):** Light on shorter than off; fewer than 50 flashes per minute. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Occulting (Oc):** Light on longer than off (darkness shorter than light). [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Isophase (Iso):** Equal duration of light and darkness. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Quick (Q):** 50–79 flashes per minute. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Very Quick (VQ):** 80–159 flashes per minute. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Long Flash (LFl):** A single flash of 2 seconds or more; used in safe water mark characteristic LFl 10s. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Morse Code A (Mo(A)):** Short-long flash (· —); used exclusively on safe water marks. [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]
- **Alternating (Al):** Light alternates between two colours (e.g. Al Oc Bu Y). [Source: SRC-003, Light Characteristics]

### Sector Lights and Leading Lights

- A sector light shows different colours in different arcs; the white sector typically indicates the safe approach track; the red sector indicates danger or being off course. [Source: Transcript p.31]
- Leading lights are two lights (upper and lower) positioned so that when vertically aligned they indicate the vessel is on the correct leading line into harbour. If the upper light appears to the left of the lower, the vessel is too far to starboard; if to the right, too far to port. [Source: Transcript p.31]
- A light characteristic notation example: Iso.WR.4s 20m 10M/7M — isophase, white and red sectors, 4-second period, 20 m above MHWS, white sector visible 10 M, red sector visible 7 M. [Source: Transcript p.31]

## Safety-Critical Notes

- **Region B reversal — critical hazard:** In IALA Region B (Americas, Japan, South Korea, Philippines), the lateral mark colours are reversed relative to Region A. A navigator trained in Region A who treats red marks as port-hand and green as starboard-hand in Region B waters will be navigating on the wrong side of every channel. Always confirm the applicable IALA region before entering unfamiliar waters.
- **Cardinal mark confusion — South vs West:** Both South and West cardinals show yellow/black banding. The distinguishing feature at night is the long flash (LFl) appended to the South cardinal's six quick flashes. Confusing the two could lead a vessel toward danger rather than away from it.
- **Isolated danger mark — do not approach:** The isolated danger mark is placed directly over the danger. "Navigable water all around" means the surrounding water is clear, not that the mark itself is safe to approach. Pass well clear.
- **Topmarks degraded by weather:** Weed, guano, and solar panels can obscure or alter the appearance of topmarks in real conditions. Do not rely solely on topmark identification; cross-check with light characteristics and chart. [Source: Transcript p.30]
- **Emergency wreck marking buoy — not on chart:** By definition, an emergency wreck marking buoy marks a danger that is not yet charted. If one is encountered, the wreck beneath or nearby is uncharted and the immediate area should be avoided.
- **Fl(2+1) reserved for preferred channel marks:** The composite group flashing rhythm is used exclusively for junction marks. Any other mark showing Fl(2+1) would be anomalous; investigate before proceeding.
