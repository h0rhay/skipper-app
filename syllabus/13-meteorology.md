# Meteorology

## Summary

Understanding weather is fundamental to safe passage planning. Day Skipper students must be able to obtain and interpret marine weather forecasts (including the Shipping Forecast), understand pressure systems, fronts, and their associated weather, interpret the Beaufort wind scale, recognise land and sea breeze effects, and understand the significance of deteriorating weather for a vessel underway.

## Key Facts

- Low and high pressure systems are the primary drivers of weather in UK and Northern European waters. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- In the Northern Hemisphere: low pressure systems (depressions) revolve anticlockwise; high pressure systems (anticyclones) revolve clockwise. In the Southern Hemisphere the directions are reversed. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- Isobars are lines on a synoptic (weather) chart connecting points of equal atmospheric pressure. The closer together the isobars, the stronger the wind. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- A depression (LOW) typically brings: cloud, rain (especially near fronts), poor visibility, backing winds ahead of a warm front, veering winds as a cold front passes; isolated squalls behind the cold front. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- A high pressure system (HIGH) typically brings: settled, fair weather; lighter winds. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- A warm front is shown on a synoptic chart as a red line with semicircles pointing in the direction of movement. A cold front is shown as a blue line with triangles. An occluded front uses both symbols combined. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34]
- Backing: wind changing direction anticlockwise (e.g., NW to SW). Veering: wind changing direction clockwise (e.g., NE to SE). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- A gale warning is issued when the average wind is expected to be Force 6 or more, or gusts 43–51 knots. Force 6 is often called a "yachtsman's gale". [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Forecast timing terms: Imminent = within 6 hours of issue; Soon = within 6–12 hours; Later = more than 12 hours from issue. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Visibility terms: Good = greater than 5 miles; Moderate = 2–5 miles; Poor = 1,000 m to 2 miles; Fog = less than 1,000 m. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Beaufort Force 4 (Moderate, 11–16 knots): frequent white horses; consider reducing headsail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Beaufort Force 5 (Fresh, 17–21 knots): moderate waves, many white crests; reef the mainsail. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Beaufort Force 6 (Strong breeze, 22–27 knots): large waves, white foam crests; reef main and reduce headsail; displacement speed for power vessels. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Beaufort Force 7 (Near gale, 28–33 knots): sea heaps up, spray, breaking waves, foam blows in streaks; deep-reefed main, small jib. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Beaufort Force 8 (Gale, 34–40 knots): moderately high waves, breaking crests; deep-reefed main, storm jib. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Beaufort Force 10 (Storm, 48–55 knots): very high waves with long breaking crests — survival conditions. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Shipping Forecast areas around UK waters include (from the transcript): Viking, North Utsire, South Utsire, Forties, Cromarty, Forth, Tyne, Dogger, Fisher, German Bight, Humber, Thames, Dover, Wight, Portland, Plymouth, Biscay, FitzRoy, Sole, Fastnet, Irish Sea, Lundy, Shannon, Rockall, Malin, Hebrides, Bailey, Fair Isle, Faeroes, Southeast Iceland. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Ways to obtain a marine weather forecast: VHF Coastguard broadcasts (Marine Safety Information); Metfax; recorded phone forecasts; SMS; internet; Teletext; local radio; harbour/marina notice boards; almanac (for times and details). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Sea breeze: in fair weather with light to moderate offshore wind, warm air rises over land, cools, descends and blows onshore. Wind up to Force 4. Develops by day. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Land breeze: on clear nights, cool air descends over land and flows out to sea (especially from river estuaries). Usually no more than Force 2–3 except near mountains. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35]
- Wind over tide (wind opposing tidal stream) creates short, steep waves disproportionate to the wind strength — more dangerous than wind-with-tide conditions of the same force. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Calmer conditions can be found in the lee of the land (sheltered from wind). [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Isobar | A line on a synoptic chart connecting points of equal atmospheric pressure | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Front | The boundary between two air masses of different temperature and humidity | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Warm front | The leading edge of a warm air mass advancing over colder air; shown as red line with semicircles | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Cold front | The leading edge of a cold air mass advancing under warm air; shown as blue line with triangles; brings squally weather | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Occlusion | A front formed when a cold front catches and lifts a warm front off the surface | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Trough | An elongated area of lower pressure extending from a depression; often associated with a line of cloud, showers, or squally winds | SRC-001 |
| Ridge | An elongated area of higher pressure between two depressions; typically brings a brief improvement | SRC-001 |
| Beaufort force | A standardised scale (0–12) describing wind speed and sea state | SRC-001; transcript page-36 |
| Synoptic chart | A weather map showing isobars, fronts, and pressure systems at a given time | SRC-001; transcript page-34 |
| Backing | Wind changing direction anticlockwise (e.g., NW to SW) | SRC-001; transcript page-35 |
| Veering | Wind changing direction clockwise (e.g., NE to SE) | SRC-001; transcript page-35 |
| Wind over tide | A condition where wind direction opposes the tidal stream direction, creating short steep dangerous waves | SRC-001; transcript page-36 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: In the Northern Hemisphere, which direction does wind circulate around a low pressure system, and which around a high? | A: Low (depression): anticlockwise. High (anticyclone): clockwise. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34
- Q: What are the Beaufort Force 5 characteristics? | A: Fresh breeze, 17–21 knots; moderate waves with many white crests; sailing vessels should reef the mainsail. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36
- Q: What is the difference between "backing" and "veering"? | A: Backing = wind changing anticlockwise (e.g., NW → SW). Veering = wind changing clockwise (e.g., NE → SE). | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35
- Q: What is the visibility category "Poor" in a shipping forecast? | A: 1,000 m to 2 nautical miles. (Fog = less than 1,000 m.) | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35
- Q: What is "wind over tide" and why is it dangerous? | A: When the wind opposes the tidal stream, creating short, steep waves out of proportion to the wind strength — more hazardous than equivalent wind-with-tide conditions. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36
- Q: What triggers a gale warning? | A: Average wind Force 6 or above, or gusts of 43–51 knots. Force 6 is sometimes called a "yachtsman's gale". | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35

## MCQ Candidates

Q: In the Northern Hemisphere, a depression is approaching from the west. As the warm front passes over your position, what typically happens to the wind direction?
(a) It backs (shifts anticlockwise)
(b) It veers (shifts clockwise)
(c) It remains steady
(d) It increases to gale force
Correct: b
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-34

Q: A weather forecast describes conditions as "Imminent." How soon is the forecast event expected?
(a) More than 12 hours from the time of issue
(b) Within 6–12 hours of issue
(c) Within 6 hours of issue
(d) Immediately, already occurring
Correct: c
Source: SRC-001; transcript page-35

Q: Which Beaufort Force is associated with "large waves, white foam crests" and is the threshold usually described as "Strong breeze"?
(a) Force 5
(b) Force 6
(c) Force 7
(d) Force 8
Correct: b

Q: You are planning to enter a bay in the afternoon. The forecast shows a light northerly offshore wind, sunshine, and no frontal activity. What local weather effect should you anticipate?
(a) Land breeze — cool air flowing out to sea
(b) Sea breeze — warm air rising over land, onshore flow up to Force 4
(c) A trough bringing rain and backing winds
(d) No local effect — the synoptic pattern dominates
Correct: b

Q: Isobars are closely spaced on a synoptic chart. What does this indicate?
(a) A large high pressure system bringing settled weather
(b) Light and variable winds
(c) Strong winds in that area
(d) Dense fog is likely
Correct: c

## Safety-Critical Notes

- Wind over tide (opposing wind and tidal stream) creates short, steep, breaking waves significantly more dangerous than the wind force alone would suggest. When wind is against a strong spring tide, even Force 4–5 can produce hazardous sea states. Plan passages to avoid wind-over-tide at key headlands or tidal gates. [SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- At the first sign of deteriorating weather: obtain an updated forecast; consider altering course for a safe haven; inform crew; brief them on safety procedures; reduce sail early. It is always easier to make decisions early than in worsening conditions. [SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- A gale warning (Force 6+) issued for a sea area means conditions are likely to be unsuitable for small craft. Day Skipper students should not put to sea when a gale warning is in force for their intended area. [SRC-001; transcript page-35]
