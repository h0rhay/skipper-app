# Passage Planning

## Summary

Passage planning is the process of preparing systematically for a voyage — from initial appraisal through to arrival. Good passage planning considers the navigational environment, weather, tidal windows, hazards, and contingency options. It includes making a pilotage plan for the inshore parts of the passage, setting waypoints, briefing the crew, and maintaining navigational records throughout. SOLAS Chapter V requires all vessels (including recreational craft) to plan passages.

## Key Facts

- Pilotage is the art of inshore navigation using visual references — transits, charted marks, clearing bearings, depth contours — to navigate along the coast and in and out of harbour. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32]
- Good passage planning allows the skipper to navigate from the deck rather than spending time below. A well-made plan means the skipper can act immediately rather than reacting. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32]
- Pilotage techniques include: transits (two objects in line, giving an accurate position line); depth contours (can be followed in poor visibility — allow for height of tide); back bearings (bearing taken astern to a known charted object); clearing bearings (bearing that keeps the vessel clear of a hazard — e.g., "not less than 043°(M)"); bearing and distance from a mark; turning points defined by a bearing on a charted object. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32]
- Clearing bearings define a safe corridor: the vessel may go anywhere between two prescribed bearings and remain clear of hazards. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32]
- A pilotage plan should include a sequence of: transits; contours (with tidal height allowance); depth over bars; positive identification of buoys and marks; back bearings; clearing lines. Bearings should be recorded in Compass (°C) or Magnetic (°M) for immediate use on deck. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-33]
- The key elements of pilotage plan execution: (1) transit; (2) contour (depth sounder); (3) clearance over bar (tidal height check); (4) positive ID of marks; (5) back bearing; (6) clearing lines. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-33]
- Weather preparation: obtain a forecast before departure; assess how the forecast will affect the passage; consider wind direction relative to the planned route (downwind passage is comfortable; returning into wind is uncomfortable); be prepared to alter plans. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Tidal planning: identify HW/LW times and heights at the destination and at critical waypoints; calculate tidal streams for each leg of the passage (using tidal diamonds or tidal stream atlas); plan to have a fair (favourable) tide where possible, particularly at tidal gates and harbour bars. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Waypoints (WPTs) are stored GPS positions used to plan and execute the passage track. Plot all waypoints on the chart before departure. Always check that the GPS distance and bearing to each waypoint match the charted values. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- Do not place a waypoint on a hazard — place it adjacent to hazards so the vessel passes safely. In busy areas, many vessels may be converging on the same popular waypoint. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- GPS alone is not sufficient for passage navigation — back up GPS with visual bearings, depth information, and the ship's log. Keep a record of positions at regular intervals in the ship's log. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-27]
- Things to plan for on a pilotage: rocks exposed/covered at different tidal heights; shoals and shallows; shipping channels; chain ferries; tidal effect on track; harbour bylaws; speed restrictions; small craft channels; change of buoyage system. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32]
- Watchkeeping: maintaining a proper look-out is required at all times underway (COLREGS Rule 5). The ship's log (deck log) should record: time, position, course, speed, log reading, weather, and any significant events. [Source: SRC-001; colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 5]
- Sailing upwind is more demanding and seems windier than sailing downwind; plan crew rest accordingly and be conservative about offshore legs when the forecast shows deteriorating weather. [Source: SRC-001; transcript page-36]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Waypoint (WPT) | A position stored in the GPS, used to define the planned track | SRC-001; transcript page-28 |
| Track | The planned line on the chart from departure to destination (or between waypoints) | SRC-001 |
| XTE | Cross-Track Error — the GPS-derived perpendicular distance the vessel is from the planned track | SRC-001 |
| ETA | Estimated Time of Arrival at a destination or waypoint | SRC-001 |
| ETD | Estimated Time of Departure | SRC-001 |
| ETE | Estimated Time En route | SRC-001 |
| Watch system | A rota system for dividing the crew into groups that take turns on deck, ensuring the vessel is always manned while some crew rest | SRC-001 |
| Deck log | The navigational record kept underway; records position, course, speed, log reading, weather, and significant events | SRC-001; transcript page-27 |
| Clearing bearing | A bearing that, when maintained (e.g., "not less than 043°M"), keeps the vessel clear of a specific hazard | SRC-001; transcript page-32 |
| Transit | Two charted objects observed in line from the vessel; provides an accurate position line or turning point | SRC-001; transcript page-32 |
| Pilotage | Inshore navigation using visual references (transits, buoys, clearing bearings, depth contours) | SRC-001; transcript page-32 |

## Flashcard Candidates

- Q: What are the six pilotage techniques described in the RYA Day Skipper material? | A: (1) Transits; (2) following depth contours; (3) back bearings; (4) clearing bearings; (5) bearing and distance from a mark; (6) turning points defined by a bearing. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32
- Q: What is a clearing bearing and how is it used? | A: A bearing that, when maintained (e.g., "keep not less than 043°M"), ensures the vessel remains clear of a specified hazard. Two clearing bearings can define a safe corridor between two hazards. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32
- Q: What should you check immediately after entering a waypoint into a GPS? | A: Compare the bearing and distance shown by the GPS with the bearing and distance you have measured on the chart. Any discrepancy means the waypoint was probably entered incorrectly. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-28
- Q: Why should bearings in a pilotage plan be written in Magnetic or Compass rather than True? | A: Because the skipper and helmsman use these bearings directly on deck with the ship's compass or hand-bearing compass, which work in Magnetic or Compass — converting True at sea under pressure leads to errors. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-33
- Q: Name four things a Day Skipper should plan for when making a pilotage plan. | A: Any four from: rocks exposed at different states of tide; shoals and shallows; shipping channels; chain ferries; tidal effect on track; harbour bylaws; speed restrictions in channels; small craft channels; change of buoyage system. | Source: SRC-001; transcript page-32
- Q: What does XTE stand for, and what does it tell you? | A: Cross-Track Error — the perpendicular distance from the vessel's GPS position to the planned track. A large XTE means the vessel has been pushed off track, e.g., by tide or leeway. | Source: SRC-001

## Safety-Critical Notes

- A proper look-out must be maintained at all times underway. COLREGS Rule 5: "Every vessel shall at all times maintain a proper look-out by sight and hearing as well as by all available means appropriate in the prevailing circumstances and conditions so as to make a full appraisal of the situation and of the risk of collision." [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 5]
- Never place a waypoint directly on a hazard — always offset it to pass safely. Steering direct to a waypoint without prior cross-tide calculation can set the vessel toward the hazard. [SRC-001; transcript page-28]
- Always obtain an up-to-date weather forecast before departure and at regular intervals during a passage. Be prepared to divert to a safe haven if conditions deteriorate. [SRC-001; transcript page-36]
- Wind over tide at harbour entrances, bars, and tidal gates can create steep, dangerous seas even in moderate wind strengths. Plan passage timing to avoid these conditions. [SRC-001; transcript page-36]
