# Topic 15 — Navigation in Restricted Visibility

> **SOURCE:** `research/colregs-irpcs-text.md` — USCG Navigation Center amalgamated rules, retrieved 2026-03-17. Rule 19 and Rule 35 text now available; Unverified section resolved.

---

## Summary

Restricted visibility occurs whenever visibility is reduced by fog, mist, falling snow, heavy rain, sandstorms, or any other similar cause. It is one of the most hazardous conditions a skipper can encounter because:

- Other vessels may not be seen until dangerously close.
- Radar targets may be present that are not visible to the eye.
- Sound signals carry over distance but can be directionally misleading — echoes from cliffs and other vessels distort apparent bearings.
- Crew awareness, fatigue, and stress levels all increase markedly.

**The RYA Day Skipper syllabus requires working knowledge of:**
- The precautions to take before and during navigation in restricted visibility.
- The limitations imposed on the vessel and skipper by poor visibility.
- IRPCS Rule 19 — the specific rule governing conduct in restricted visibility (detailed knowledge required).

**Practical seamanship approach:** The primary precaution is to reduce speed to the minimum needed to maintain steerage way, maintain a proper lookout using all available senses and instruments, post extra crew on watch, make the required sound signals, plot and track all radar contacts, and — where safe — consider anchoring to wait for conditions to improve.

A vessel entering or caught in fog should immediately: reduce speed, post a dedicated look-out forward, switch on navigation lights (if not already lit), commence making fog signals, obtain and monitor a fix, brief the crew on the situation, and prepare emergency equipment.

If another vessel is heard (fog signal) or detected on radar apparently ahead, the rules require a reduction of speed to minimum steerage way. The general principle is: do not cross ahead of another vessel detected forward of the beam.

## Key Facts

*(Seamanship facts below are now supplemented by Rule 19 and Rule 35 text from colregs-irpcs-text.md.)*

- In restricted visibility, a proper lookout should be kept using ALL available means — sight, sound, and radar if fitted. Relying on visual watch alone is insufficient. [Source: transcript page-15 (general lookout principle)]
- A vessel should proceed at a SAFE SPEED at all times, and in restricted visibility this means reducing speed so that the vessel can stop within the distance of visibility. [Source: transcript page-15 (safe speed principle)]
- Blind spots caused by sails, sprayhoods, and dodgers are particularly dangerous in restricted visibility — a dedicated look-out should be stationed forward, clear of obstructions. [Source: transcript page-15]
- Navigation lights must be switched on in restricted visibility, regardless of whether it is day or night. A sailing vessel of less than 20 m may use a tricolour masthead light OR separate sidelights and sternlight. The tricolour must NOT be used simultaneously with a steaming light. [Source: transcript page-17]
- A sailing vessel motoring in fog must display a steaming light (masthead), sidelights, and sternlight — and must display the motoring cone by day. She is treated as a power-driven vessel. [Source: transcript page-17]
- All fog signals (sound signals in restricted visibility) must be made at regular intervals; these are mandatory, not optional. [Source: transcript page-16 context; specific signals — see Unverified]
- If anchoring to wait out fog, a vessel must make the correct anchoring sound signal at regular intervals and must carry an anchor ball by day. [Source: transcript page-17 (anchor ball = black ball day shape, all-round white light at night)]
- Taking a fix and recording a running estimated position at frequent intervals is essential in fog to track the vessel's position relative to hazards. [Source: general seamanship — see Unverified for regulatory basis]
- Radar, if fitted, should be set to the shortest appropriate range to detect targets early. The absence of a radar contact does not guarantee clear water — small vessels and debris may not return a strong echo. [Source: general seamanship — see Unverified for regulatory basis]
- In any emergency in fog (or otherwise), the skipper must ensure all crew are wearing lifejackets and that harnesses are available. [Source: transcript page-12 (general emergency precaution): "Get everyone on deck with a lifejacket"]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|------------|--------|
| Restricted visibility | Any condition in which visibility is reduced by fog, mist, falling snow, heavy rain, sandstorms, or any other similar cause. | colregs-irpcs-text.md — verify Rule 3(l) |
| Fog signal | A sound signal made at regular prescribed intervals by a vessel in or near an area of restricted visibility, to warn other vessels of its presence. | colregs-irpcs-text.md — verify Rule 35 |
| Safe speed | A speed at which the vessel can take proper and effective action to avoid collision and stop within the distance appropriate to the prevailing circumstances and visibility. | colregs-irpcs-text.md — verify Rule 6 |
| Minimum steerage way | The lowest speed at which a vessel retains directional control. In restricted visibility, a vessel hearing a fog signal forward of the beam must reduce to this speed. | colregs-irpcs-text.md — verify Rule 19 |
| Radar | Radio Detection And Ranging — an electronic instrument that detects targets by sending and receiving reflected radio pulses. Essential for collision avoidance in restricted visibility. | general |
| ARPA | Automatic Radar Plotting Aid — a radar function that automatically tracks targets and calculates their course, speed, and closest point of approach (CPA). | general |
| CPA | Closest Point of Approach — the nearest distance a target vessel will reach relative to own vessel if both maintain current courses and speeds. Used in radar collision avoidance. | general |
| Estimated Position (EP) | The vessel's best estimate of its position based on dead reckoning from the last known fix, accounting for course, speed, and tidal stream. More uncertain than a fix; critical to maintain in fog. | general seamanship |

## ⚠️ Safety-Critical Notes

- In fog, do NOT increase speed to "get clear quickly" — this is one of the most common and dangerous errors. Speed must be REDUCED.
- Never assume that because you cannot see another vessel, it cannot see or hit you — a large ship may appear within seconds of its fog signal being audible.
- Sound signals in fog are mandatory. Failing to make fog signals, or making them incorrectly, can contribute directly to a collision.
- A radar is only as useful as the person interpreting it — a beginner should seek additional training in radar collision avoidance (ARPA and plotting).
- Do NOT alter course to port for a vessel detected forward of the beam in restricted visibility (Rule 19 prohibition).
- If in any doubt about safety in fog, the prudent action is to anchor clear of shipping lanes, make the correct sound signals, and wait for conditions to improve.

## Additional Rule Detail (from colregs-irpcs-text.md)

**Rule 19 key provisions** [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 19]
- Applies to vessels not in sight of one another in or near restricted visibility.
- Every vessel shall proceed at safe speed; engines ready for immediate manoeuvre.
- Radar contacts: if close-quarters or collision risk developing, take avoiding action in ample time.
- Do NOT alter course to port for a vessel forward of the beam (except vessel being overtaken).
- On hearing fog signal forward of the beam: reduce to minimum steerage way; if necessary stop; navigate with extreme caution until danger past.

**Rule 35 fog signals** [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 35]
- Power-driven vessel making way: — (one prolonged blast) every 2 min
- Power-driven vessel stopped: — — (two prolonged blasts) every 2 min
- Sailing / fishing / restricted / constrained / towing: — •• (one prolonged + two short) every 2 min
- Vessel at anchor: rapid bell ring ~5 seconds every 1 min; over 100 m adds gong aft
- Vessel aground: 3 strokes + rapid ring + 3 strokes on bell

**Rule 6 safe speed factors** [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 6]
Visibility, traffic density, manoeuvrability, background light, wind/sea/current, proximity of hazards, draught vs depth. Radar-equipped vessels also consider: radar range scale, effect of sea state/weather on radar, small targets, number and density of contacts.

**Rule 7 / radar in restricted visibility** [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 7]
Radar shall be used if fitted. Proper use includes long-range scanning for early warning and radar plotting or equivalent systematic observation. Assumptions shall not be made on scanty radar information.

**Rule 20 — lights in restricted visibility** [colregs-irpcs-text.md, Rule 20]
Navigation lights must be exhibited from sunset to sunrise in all weathers, and during daylight hours in restricted visibility.
