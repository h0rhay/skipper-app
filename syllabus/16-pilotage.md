# Pilotage — Transits, Leading Lines, IALA Buoyage, Sailing Directions, Harbour Entry

## Summary

Pilotage is the art of inshore navigation using visual references — marks, transits, depth contours, bearings, and buoys — to navigate safely along the coast and in and out of harbour. Because the navigator is close to hazards, good advance planning is essential: a well-constructed pilotage plan allows the skipper to navigate from the deck without having to go below to work out the next move.

A pilotage plan sequences the navigational marks, bearings, depths, and turning points that will be used along a route. It incorporates the IALA buoyage system to identify channel marks, uses transits and leading lines to hold a track, clearing bearings to stay clear of danger, and tidal calculations to verify clearance over shoals and bars.

## Key Facts

### Pilotage — General Principles

- Pilotage is inshore navigation using visual references (marks, transits, bearings, buoys, depth contours) to navigate safely in coastal and harbour waters. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Because hazards are close, time available to react is short; a pilotage plan prepared in advance allows deck-based navigation without going below. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- A different port may use a different buoyage system — e.g. a vessel moving between IALA A and IALA B regions must treat all lateral marks as reversed. [Source: Transcript p.32; SRC-003]

### Transits

- A transit is formed when two charted objects (marks, beacons, landmarks) are observed to be in line from the vessel's position. A transit defines a precise bearing line on the chart. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Transits are the most accurate pilotage technique because they require no instrument: when the two objects are in line, the vessel is on the transit bearing. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Transits are used to: confirm position at a turning point, hold a course along a channel, and verify that the vessel remains on a planned track. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- A transit bearing is worked out in advance from the chart and noted in the pilotage plan. Example from training material: follow transit 063°(M) aligned on a mast and a watchtower. [Source: Transcript p.33]

### Leading Lines and Leading Lights

- A leading line is a specific transit that leads a vessel safely through a navigable channel or into a harbour. It is often marked by two beacons or lights deliberately aligned for this purpose. [Source: Transcript p.31]
- When using leading lights, vertical alignment of the upper and lower lights indicates the vessel is on the leading line. If the upper light appears to the left of the lower, the vessel is too far to starboard; if to the right, too far to port. [Source: Transcript p.31]
- Leading lights can be used at night and in restricted visibility to maintain the correct approach track. [Source: Transcript p.31]

### Clearing Bearings

- A clearing bearing is a bearing of a charted object that, when observed to be not less than (or not more than) a given value, keeps the vessel clear of a specified danger. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Two clearing bearings can define a safe corridor: the vessel may proceed anywhere between them, provided both conditions are satisfied simultaneously. Example: "not less than 043°(M)" and "not less than 335°(M)". [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Clearing bearings are prepared from the chart in advance and entered in the pilotage plan.

### Contour Navigation

- A depth contour (isobar of depth) can be followed as a navigational track in poor visibility; the navigator watches the echo sounder and maintains a specific depth reading. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Crossing a known depth contour provides a position line; combined with a bearing or time, it can fix position. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Tidal height must be accounted for when using depth contours: the charted depth is reduced (LAT datum) and the actual depth will vary with the state of the tide. [Source: Transcript p.33]

### Back Bearings

- A back bearing is a bearing taken to a charted object astern of the vessel, used to confirm position and check that the vessel has not been set off track. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Back bearings are included in the pilotage plan as periodic position checks, particularly after a turn. [Source: Transcript p.33]

### Turning Points

- Turning points in a pilotage plan are defined by a specific event: a transit coming in line, a charted object reaching a planned bearing, or a buoy being abeam. Example: "Turn when chimney bears 320°(T)." [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Pre-planned turning points prevent the navigator from having to estimate when to alter course and reduce the risk of cutting corners near hazards.

### Pilotage Plan Construction

- A pilotage plan is a written sequential record of the marks, bearings, depths, and actions for each leg of a passage through pilotage waters, prepared before entry. [Source: Transcript p.33]
- A six-step pilotage plan structure (from training material): (1) Transit bearing; (2) Depth contour / echo sounder value; (3) Minimum depth clearance over bar (tidal calculation); (4) Positive identification and sequence of IALA buoyage marks; (5) Back bearing check; (6) Clearing bearings. [Source: Transcript p.33]
- Each waypoint should be worked out in advance so the navigator knows when and where to expect the next mark or buoy. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- A pilotage plan enables navigation from the deck — the skipper can refer to a notepad rather than going below to consult the chart. [Source: Transcript p.32]

### Hazards to Plan For

- Items requiring specific planning include: rocks (exposed/submerged depending on tide), shoals and shallows, shipping channels, chain ferries, tidal streams, harbour bylaws and speed limits, and small craft channels. [Source: Transcript p.32]
- Tidal clearance over a bar is calculated as: charted depth + height of tide at time of passage. Example from training material: minimum depth over bar = height of tide (4 m) − error correction (0.3 m) = 3.7 m. [Source: Transcript p.33]

### Use of IALA Buoyage in Pilotage

- In a buoyed channel, lateral marks identify the edges of navigable water; the pilotage plan should list each mark in sequence with its type (port/starboard), colour, and expected light characteristic so each can be positively identified before the turn. [Source: SRC-003; Transcript p.33]
- Positive identification of marks before acting is an explicit step in the pilotage plan: do not act on a mark until it has been positively identified by colour, shape, topmark, and light. [Source: Transcript p.33]
- In Region A: leave red can buoys to port and green conical buoys to starboard when entering harbour. [Source: SRC-003, Lateral Marks; Transcript p.29]
- Cardinal marks indicate the boundary of a danger; approach from the named safe side and pass on that side. [Source: SRC-003, Cardinal Marks; Transcript p.29]
- Special marks (yellow, X topmark) often mark recreational or special-use areas within or near channels; they are not navigational channel marks. [Source: SRC-003, Special Mark]

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Transit | Two charted objects observed in line from a vessel, defining a precise bearing line on the chart | Transcript p.32 |
| Leading line | A transit specifically designed to guide a vessel safely through a channel or harbour entrance; often marked by official leading marks or lights | Transcript p.31 |
| Clearing bearing | A bearing of a charted object that, when maintained at not less than (or not more than) a stated value, keeps the vessel clear of a specific danger | Transcript p.32 |
| Pilotage plan | A written sequential record of marks, bearings, depths, and turning actions for a passage through pilotage waters, prepared in advance | Transcript p.33 |
| Waypoint | A defined position along a pilotage route at which the navigator expects a specific action (course change, mark identification, bearing check) | Transcript p.33 |
| Back bearing | A compass bearing taken to a charted object astern, used as a position check or to confirm the vessel remains on track | Transcript p.32 |
| Contour | A line of equal charted depth; can be followed as a track or used to obtain a position line via the echo sounder | Transcript p.32 |
| Direction of buoyage | The conventional direction in which IALA lateral marks are assigned; generally from seaward toward harbour | SRC-003 |
| Height of tide | The vertical distance of the water surface above chart datum at a given time; added to charted depth to determine actual depth | Transcript p.33 |

## Safety-Critical Notes

- **Never go below during pilotage:** A core principle is deck-based navigation. Going below to consult the chart removes the navigator's situational awareness precisely when it is most needed. All plan elements must be pre-prepared and accessible on deck.
- **Positive identification before acting:** Do not turn or alter course on a buoy until it has been positively identified by colour, shape, topmark, and light characteristic. Acting on the wrong mark in a buoyed channel can lead a vessel onto the hazard the mark was warning about.
- **Region A / Region B lateral mark reversal:** A vessel transiting from European (Region A) to American or Japanese (Region B) waters, or vice versa, must reverse its treatment of all lateral marks. Misidentifying the applicable region puts the vessel on the wrong side of every channel mark.
- **Tidal clearance is time-critical:** A positive clearance calculation applies only at the time it was computed. If the pilotage is delayed, recalculate bar clearance for the revised time of passage — particularly on a falling tide.
- **Contour following requires continuous echo-sounder watch:** Tidal height changes the actual depth continuously. A contour followed at HW may not be followable at LW. Always apply the predicted height of tide to the charted depth before committing to a contour track.
- **Shipping channels and chain ferries:** Vessels in confined waters may be constrained and unable to manoeuvre. A pilotage plan must identify any crossing points with commercial traffic and decide on timing and VHF contact strategy before entering the channel.
