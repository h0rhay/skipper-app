### STANDARD PORTS

Standard ports – tide tables are produced for larger ports and give times and heights of high and low water for each day of the year. Tide times may need correcting for local changes, such as: differences in time zone from Universal Time (UT) and in countries operating Daylight Saving Time (BST in UK).

#### Tidal curves

Use these for finding out depth of water at any time between high and low water.

To find out if a certain day is on springs or neaps subtract LW from HW to give the range:
5.2m – 0.6m = 4.7m

Compare with mean range on curve: Tues 16th April = springs

For springs use solid line.
For neaps use dotted line.

### SECONDARY PORTS

Secondary ports – tide tables are not produced for minor ports. To find the height and times of HW and LW at these secondary ports you will need to apply corrections, usually found in an almanac, to the nearest standard port.

#### Height difference

Height difference – when HW at St Malo is 12.2m, it is 1.6m less at Carteret. When HW is 9.2m, it is 1.1m less at Carteret.

However if HW and LW times fall between these set times you will need to interpolate between the differences figures –

e.g. if HW St. Malo is at 1630 what time is HW Carteret?

Time difference – if HW St. Malo is at 0100 or 1300 HW Carteret is 35 minutes later, but when HW St. Malo is at 0800 or 2000, HW Carteret is 25 minutes later.

To find the height of tide between HW and LW at a secondary port use a tidal curve in the same way as for a standard port.
Use the related standard port.
For example to find tidal information for Carteret you should use the curve for St. Malo.
