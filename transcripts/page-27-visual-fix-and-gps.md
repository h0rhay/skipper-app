## Content

### A VISUAL FIX

#### Three point fix

Take bearings on charted objects to fix your position.

Draw the bearings on the chart.

Bearings rarely line up as a perfect fix.

Your position will be where the bearings are closest. Use closer objects for greater accuracy.

If bearings are too close together – error is greater.

Don't use objects that will give a poor angle of cut.

Error produces a cocked hat.

#### Transit and bearing

Line up two charted objects to make a transit – this gives you a very accurate position line.
Obtain a fix by taking a bearing on another object, preferably at about 90° to the transit.

[Diagram labels:]
chimney and flag staff
070°(T)

#### Bearing and contour

Fix your position by taking a bearing on a charted object as you cross a charted contour – don't forget to allow for the height of tide.

#### The simplest fix

Plot your position as you pass a charted object.

### A GPS FIX

The simplest way to plot your position is to use the Latitude and Longitude given on the display.

A GPS receiver obtains a fix by triangulating from orbiting satellites: this gives a position which is accurate to about 15 metres.

It can also give:
- your current course and speed over the ground
- information about your position in relation to waypoints (see next page).

GPS is generally reliable and accurate but, as with all electronics, it can go wrong. The main things that can affect it are:
- power or aerial failure
- transmissions from mobile phones
- interruption or changes to the satellite system.

Always back up your GPS position with information from another source such as:

Bearing.

Keep a record of your position at regular intervals on the chart and in the ship's log.

[Secondary source illustrations:]
- IALA B buoyage
- A charted object.
- Depth allowing for tide.
